Unit III Core Strategy Overview

Weightage: ~22-26 Questions Focus Area: Multi-Statement Assertions & Chronology

Unit III anchors the General Studies paper of the TNPSC Group 1 Services. It synthesizes the entire spectrum of Indian history with special emphasis on systemic continuity, socio-political undercurrents, leadership modalities, and the evolutionary lineage of modern Tamil Nadu's political thoughts (intertwined with the National Movement).

[TIP] Trends from 2023 to 2025 confirm that linear single-choice factual recall has plummeted. Over 65% of historical questions are built around multi-statement compliance checks, match-the-following text correlations (e.g., matching obscure administrative terms to their offices), or rigorous chronology maps.

1. Indus Valley Civilization (IVC)

Typical Count: 1-2 Qs Complexity Grid: Medium to Trap

Analysis of urban structural engineering, trade routes, technological innovations, and site-specific archeological discoveries.

Recursive Breakdown Structure
1. Indus Valley Civilization
1.1 Geography & Settlement Zones
1.1.1 Core Sites: Harappa (Ravi), Mohenjo-daro (Indus), Lothal (Bhogavo), Kalibangan (Ghaggar), Dholavira, Rakhigarhi, Banawali, Chanhudaro, Sutkagendor.
→ Exam Angle: Absolute geographic coordinates, pairing sites with original excavators (e.g., Sahni, Banerjee, Rao, Bisht) and current state locations.
1.2 Town Planning & Urban Infrastructure
1.2.1 Citadel vs Lower Town layouts, advanced grid iron road patterns, burnt brick specifications (1:2:4 ratio), underground integrated sanitary networks, Great Bath engineering, Granary designs (Harappa).
→ Exam Angle: Analytical multi-statement verification on plumbing technologies, multi-story structural layouts, absence of dedicated temples.
1.3 Socio-Economic Profile & External Trade Ties
1.3.1 Agricultural base (wheat, barley, cotton), animal domestication (humped bull; lack of definitive horse evidence until upper layers), weight systems (binary 1, 2, 4, 8 to 64, then decimal), seal manufacturing (steatite, unicorn iconography), Mesopotamian cuneiform references to "Meluhha".
→ Exam Angle: Trade materials mapping (Lapis Lazuli from Badakhshan, Jade from Central Asia, Turquoise from Persia).
1.4 Epigraphy, Art, and Theories of Decline
1.4.1 Boustrophedon script layout (right to left, left to right), Priest-King stone sculpture, Bronze Dancing Girl (lost-wax casting technique), Pashupati Mahadeva seal details (surrounding animals: elephant, tiger, rhino, buffalo, two deer at feet).
→ Exam Angle: Decipherment arguments (Asko Parpola / Iravatham Mahadevan Dravidian affinity hypotheses) and terminal decline reasons.

Q3. Assess the linguistic arguments presented by epigraphist Iravatham Mahadevan regarding the Indus Valley Script:

Reveal Evaluation Matrix & Core Verification
Correct Answer: B. Iravatham Mahadevan's monumental work relied on structural syntax models showing Dravidian patterns (suffix-root alignment), rejecting both purely alphabetic and early Indo-European connections.

2. Guptas, Delhi Sultans, Mughals, and Marathas

Typical Count: 4-5 Qs Complexity Grid: Hard Chronology

A systematic comparative analysis tracing changes and continuity in administration, taxation, military structures, and architectural achievements across these empires.

Recursive Breakdown Structure
2. Empire Dynasties & Administrative Continuities
2.1 The Gupta Empire Architecture
2.1.1 Land Taxonomy: Kshetra (cultivable), Khila (wasteland), Aprahata (forest), Vasti (habitable), Gopata Sarah (pasture). Epigraphic sources: Allahabad Pillar (Prashasti by Harisena), Mehrauli Iron Pillar.
→ Exam Angle: Match-the-following land classes, coins (Dinara, Rupaka), and structural changes under Kumargupta/Skandagupta (Huna invasions).
2.2 The Delhi Sultanate (1206-1526)
2.2.1 Administrative Ministries: Diwan-i-Wizarat (Finance), Diwan-i-Arz (Military - Balban), Diwan-i-Insha (Correspondence), Diwan-i-Rasalat (Foreign/Religious). Agrarian systems: Iqta assignments, Khalisa crowns lands, Alaudin Khilji’s market pricing machinery (Shahna-i-Mandi).
→ Exam Angle: Chronological placement of dynasties (Mamluk, Khilji, Tughlaq, Sayyid, Lodi), administrative terminology, and token currency experiments.
2.3 The Mughal Empire (1526-1707)
2.3.1 Revenue Machinery: Raja Todar Mal’s Dahsala Framework, Zabti layout. Military Ordering: Mansabdari structure (Zat and Sawar ranks), Du-aspa Sih-aspa innovations under Jahangir. Architecture: Evolution from Humayun's tomb to the Pietra Dura inlay works of Shah Jahan.
→ Exam Angle: Step-by-step chronology of text compilations (Ain-i-Akbari, Muntakhab-ut-Tawarikh) and specific religious declarations (Mazhar nama 1579).
2.4 The Maratha Confederacy
2.4.1 Chhatrapati Shivaji’s Statecraft: Ashtapradhan Ministry framework (Peshwa, Amatya, Wakia-Navis, Samant, Sachiv, Pandit Rao, Nyayadhish, Senapati). Taxation: Chauth (1/4th revenue as protection fee) and Sardeshmukhi (additional 10% as hereditary claim).
→ Exam Angle: Treaties progression (Purandar 1665, Surat campaigns) and the shift of power matrix to Peshwas.

3. Age of Vijayanagaram and Bahmani Kingdoms

Typical Count: 2 Qs Complexity Grid: Hard Multi-Statement

Traces the geopolitical rivalries in the Krishna-Tungabhadra doab, the evolution of the Nayankara system, and architectural developments.

Recursive Breakdown Structure
3. Vijayanagar and Bahmani Geopolitics
3.1 Vijayanagar Structural Lineage (1336-1646)
3.1.1 Four Royal Houses: Sangama (Harihara, Bukka), Saluva (Narasimha), Tuluva (Krishna Deva Raya), Aravidu (Tirumala). Administrative framework: The **Nayankara System** (military chiefs holding territory or 'Amaram' lands in exchange for maintaining troop contingencies).
→ Exam Angle: Specific analytical insights into Krishna Deva Raya’s reign, his administrative monograph *Amuktamalyada*, and architectural features like the Kalyan Mandapa.
3.2 The Bahmani Sultanate Core (1347-1527)
3.2.1 Founding: Alauddin Hasan Bahman Shah. Institutional Reforms of **Mahmud Gawan**: division of the kingdom into 8 administrative provinces (Tarafs), introduction of land measurement, and building the Bidar Madrasa library.
→ Exam Angle: Provincial governor titles (e.g., Malik-naib) and the division into 5 successor states (Bijapur, Ahmednagar, Berar, Golconda, Bidar).
3.3 Foreign Epigraphic Accounts & Terminal Clashes
3.3.1 Key Accounts: Nicolo de Conti (Sangama era), Abdur Razzaq (Devaraya II), Domingos Paes and Fernao Nuniz (Krishna Deva Raya). Battle of **Talikota / Rakshasi-Tangadi (1565)**: Rama Raya's defeat by the Decan Confederacy alliance.
→ Exam Angle: Matching foreign travelers with the exact monarch they visited.

4. South Indian History — Socio-Cultural Change & Continuity

Typical Count: 5-6 Qs Complexity Grid: Hard Local Terms

Deals with the evolution of South Indian society from the Sangam Era, through the Pallavas, Imperial Cholas, and Later Pandyas, tracking shifts in land, temple economies, and cultural institutions.

Recursive Breakdown Structure
4. South Indian History Evolution Architecture
4.1 Pallavas of Kanchi & Rock Art Shifts
4.1.1 Structural Styles: Mahendravarman group (rock-cut caves), Mamalla style (monolithic Rathas at Mahabalipuram), Rajasimha design (structural stone temples like the Kailasanathar at Kanchi). Socio-religious: The rise of the **Bhakti Movement** driven by Nayanmars and Alvars.
→ Exam Angle: Royal titles (e.g., *Mattavilasa* for Mahendravarman I) and key literary works like the *Mattavilasa Prahasana*.
4.2 Imperial Chola Administrative Sub-systems (850-1279)
4.2.1 Local Governance Architecture: **Uttaramerur Inscriptions (919 and 921 AD)** during Parantaka I’s reign, detailing the *Kudavolai* (pot-ticket) ballot selection system for village committees (*Variyams*). Land Tenure: *Vellanvagai* (non-brahmin peasant holdings), *Brahmadeya* (brahmin grants), *Devadana* (temple lands), *Shalaboga* (school maintenance).
→ Exam Angle: Committee eligibility qualifications, disqualification parameters, and the specialized functions of ministries like the *Udan-kuttam*.
4.3 The Later Pandyas & Overseas Contacts
4.3.1 Administrative Units: *Valanadus* and *Nadus*. Maritime Trade Hubs: Kayalport, documented extensively by Venetian traveler Marco Polo and Persian historian Wassaf. Trade Guilds: *Manigramam* and *Ayyavole-five-hundred*.
→ Exam Angle: Economic history questions focusing on pearl fishing revenues and horse import monopolies.

5. National Renaissance — Early Uprisings Against British Rule

Typical Count: 3 Qs Complexity Grid: Hard Local Alliances

Deals with early anti-colonial resistance movements, mapping the Poligar Wars, the expansion of the Palayakkarar system, and the structural causes of the Vellore Mutiny.

Recursive Breakdown Structure
5. Early Anti-Colonial Uprisings System
5.1 The Palayakkarar Framework & Early Resistance
5.1.1 Evolution: System created by Viswanatha Nayaka of Madurai (1529) with minister Ariyanatha Mudaliar. **Puli Thevar**: First Palayakkarar to resist the British (Nawab/Company alliance), organizing the Western Bloc and securing victory at Kalakadu.
→ Exam Angle: Key operational bases (Nerkattumseval, Vasudevanallur), the role of Yusuf Khan (Marudhanayagam), and the chronological shift to the Eastern Palayams.
5.2 Veerapandiya Kattabomman & The Marudu Brothers Alliance
5.2.1 Kattabomman: The confrontation at Ramnathapuram over tax arrears with Collector Jackson, the subsequent siege by Major Bannerman, the role of spy Ramalinganar, and Kattabomman's execution at Kayathar (1799). **Marudu Pandiyar**: The **Tiruchirappalli Proclamation (June 1801)**, an early call for pan-Indian unity against Company rule.
→ Exam Angle: Analysis of the 1801 Proclamation text, the composition of the South Indian Confederation, and the resulting Carnatic Treaty of July 1801.
5.3 Dheeran Chinnamalai & The Vellore Mutiny (1806)
5.3.1 Chinnamalai: Guerrilla tactics used in the Kongu region, the Battles of Cauvery, Odanilai, and Arachalur, and his execution at Sankagiri (1805). **Vellore Mutiny (July 10, 1806)**: Triggered by Sir John Cradock's new military dress codes (Agnew turban, shaving rules), the actions of Tipu’s sons, and its suppression by Colonel Gillespie.
→ Exam Angle: Strategic timeline of the mutiny day, involved regiments (1st and 23rd), and the long-term institutional consequences.
