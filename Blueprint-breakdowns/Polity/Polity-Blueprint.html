01. Constitution of India — Preamble to the Constitution

Topic Overview

The Preamble is the identity card of the Constitution. For TNPSC Group 1, questions consistently target the precise sequential order of key phrases, the philosophical origins of ideals, and the evolving judicial stances across historic cases regarding its amendability.

Constitutional Links: Objective Resolution (Dec 13, 1946), 42nd Amendment Act (1976).

1.1 Historical Genesis
1.1.1 Objective Resolution by Nehru
→ Date of adoption: Jan 22, 1947 by Constituent Assembly
1.2 Semantic Sequencings
1.2.1 Core Sovereign Description Order
→ Sovereign, Socialist, Secular, Democratic, Republic (S-S-S-D-R pattern)
1.2.2 Justice, Liberty, Equality, Fraternity Order
→ Social, Economic, Political Justice source from Russian Revolution 1917
Year Question Type Topic Tested Article/Source Toughness Callout Tag
2022 Multi-Statement Selection 42nd Amendment additions to Preamble Objective Resolution Context Easy Direct Match
2024 Chronological Case Ordering Preamble status as part of Constitution Berubari vs Kesavananda Cases Medium Case Matrix

360° Coverage Boundaries

IN SCOPE: Source of authority, exact date of adoption (Nov 26, 1949), semantic expansion of terms, French/Russian revolution imports, full tracking of Kesavananda Bharati case mandates.

OUT OF SCOPE: Textual comparisons with non-US/non-Indian modern preambles.

[TRAP] The Fraternity Paradox TNPSC often flips the order of Justice types. Remember social comes first, economic second, political third. Also, the phrase 'Dignity of the individual and unity and integrity' was modified by 42nd CAA, adding 'Integrity'. The original text had 'Unity of the Nation' only.

Q1. Which of the following is the correct structural order of words as enshrined in the Preamble of the Indian Constitution?
  • A) Sovereign, Secular, Socialist, Democratic, Republic
  • B) Sovereign, Socialist, Secular, Democratic, Republic
  • C) Socialist, Secular, Sovereign, Democratic, Republic
  • D) Sovereign, Democratic, Socialist, Secular, Republic
Reveal Answer & Core Insights

Correct Answer: B

Reference: Preamble text as altered by the 42nd Constitutional Amendment Act, 1976.

Explanation: The exact terminology matrix follows: Sovereign, Socialist, Secular, Democratic, Republic. The terms 'Socialist', 'Secular', and 'Integrity' were added by the 42nd Amendment under the Indira Gandhi administration.

[GEM] Live Gem Insight Although the Constitution was adopted on November 26, 1949, the Preamble was enacted by the Constituent Assembly *after* the rest of the Constitution had already been passed to ensure consistency with the text.
Q2. In which historical case did the Supreme Court explicitly state that the Preamble is NOT a part of the Indian Constitution?
  • A) Berubari Union case (1960)
  • B) Kesavananda Bharati case (1973)
  • C) LIC of India case (1995)
  • D) Golaknath case (1967)
Reveal Answer & Core Insights

Correct Answer: A

Reference: Berubari Union & Exchange of Enclaves Reference, 1960.

Explanation: In 1960, the SC opined that Preamble shows the general purpose behind the constitution but is not a part of it. This was reversed in Kesavananda Bharati (1973), which held it *is* a part, and re-affirmed in LIC of India (1995) as an "integral part".

Q3. The ideals of Justice—Social, Economic, and Political—incorporated in the Indian Preamble find their direct philosophical origin in which historic event?
  • A) The French Revolution
  • B) The Russian Revolution
  • C) The American War of Independence
  • D) The Irish Constitutional Convention
Reveal Answer & Core Insights

Correct Answer: B

Reference: Global origins of the Indian Constitutional ideals.

Explanation: The ideals of Justice (Social, Economic, Political) were extracted from the Russian Revolution (1917). Conversely, Liberty, Equality, and Fraternity were extracted from the French Revolution (1789).

This structural core focuses on how the Indian Constitution uniquely balances rigidity with flexibility, merges federalism with unitary features, and extracts global functional architectures while retaining local indigenous dynamics.

Constitutional Links: Structural frameworks borrowed from the Government of India Act 1935, structural adaptations from global constitutions.

OUT OF SCOPE: Intricate details of the internal evolution of the UK unwritten constitutional jurisprudence.

Q1. Who among the following eminent political jurists described the Indian Constitution as a system of "Cooperative Federalism"?
  • A) K.C. Wheare
  • B) Granville Austin
  • C) Morris Jones
  • D) Ivor Jennings
Reveal Answer & Core Insights

Correct Answer: B

Reference: Academic observations on Indian Federalism.

Explanation: Granville Austin termed India's network as 'Cooperative Federalism'. K.C. Wheare termed it 'Quasi-Federal'. Morris Jones called it 'Bargaining Federalism'.

Q2. The concept of "Procedure Established by Law" in Article 21 of the Indian Constitution was extracted from which global constitutional system?
  • A) American Constitution
  • B) British Constitution
  • C) Japanese Constitution
  • D) Canadian Constitution
Reveal Answer & Core Insights

Correct Answer: C

Reference: Source mapping of Article 21.

Explanation: Article 21's original terminology "Procedure Established by Law" is from the Japanese Constitution. The American system uses "Due Process of Law", which the Indian SC read into Article 21 starting with the Maneka Gandhi Case (1978).

[TRAP] Target Alert Do not confuse "Procedure Established by Law" (Japan) with "Rule of Law" (Britain). TNPSC uses these interchangeably in distractors to trigger negative markings.
Q3. Which of the following features indicates the unitary bias of the Indian Constitution?
  • A) Written Constitution and Rigidity of Amendment
  • B) Single Constitution, Appointment of Governor, and Integrated Judiciary
  • C) Bicameralism and Division of Powers
  • D) Independent Judiciary and Independent Comptroller and Auditor General
Reveal Answer & Core Insights

Correct Answer: B

Reference: Unitary vs Federal features taxonomy.

Explanation: Unitary features lean power toward the center. Single Constitution, Central appointment of Governors, All India Services, and an integrated judicial hierarchy are all centralized characteristics designed to check centrifugal forces.

Q1. Under Article 3 of the Indian Constitution, a bill relating to the reorganisation or alteration of boundaries of any state can be introduced in Parliament ONLY on the prior recommendation of:
  • A) The Prime Minister of India
  • B) The Governor of the State concerned
  • C) The President of India
  • D) The Inter-State Council Chairman
Reveal Answer & Core Insights

Correct Answer: C

Reference: Constitutional constraints on Article 3 legislative pipelines.

Explanation: Article 3 contains two conditions: No bill can be introduced without the President's prior recommendation; and before recommending, the President must refer the bill to the concerned state legislature for expressing its views within a specified period.

[TRAP] State Power Myth The views of the state legislature are completely *non-binding* on the Parliament. Parliament can accept or reject the state's views, even if they unanimously oppose the state's destruction.
Q2. Which of the following represents the correct chronological sequence of the setup of committees for the linguistic reorganisation of Indian states?
  • A) JVP Committee → Dhar Commission → Fazl Ali Commission
  • B) Dhar Commission → JVP Committee → Fazl Ali Commission
  • C) Fazl Ali Commission → Dhar Commission → JVP Committee
  • D) Dhar Commission → Fazl Ali Commission → JVP Committee
Reveal Answer & Core Insights

Correct Answer: B

Reference: Historical commissions of Part I structural timeline.

Explanation: Dhar Commission was formed in June 1948. JVP Committee followed in December 1948. The Fazl Ali Commission was instituted much later in December 1953 post the death of Potti Sriramulu.

Q3. According to Article 4 of the Constitution, laws made under Articles 2 and 3 for the amendment of the First and Fourth Schedules shall:
  • A) Require a special majority under Article 368
  • B) Be passed by a simple majority as an ordinary piece of legislation
  • C) Require ratification by not less than half of the states
  • D) Require a absolute majority of total membership in Rajya Sabha
Reveal Answer & Core Insights

Correct Answer: B

Reference: Constitutional amendment exclusion paths (Article 4).

Explanation: Article 4 explicitly states that laws framing new states or changing boundaries are *not* to be considered amendments of the Constitution under Article 368. Hence, they can be carried out by a simple operational majority in Parliament.

04. Citizenship Architecture

Topic Overview

Citizenship represents the basic right to have rights. TNPSC tests Part II (Articles 5-11), modes of acquisition and termination under the Citizenship Act 1955, and contemporary iterations such as CAA 2019, OCI, and Pravasi Bharatiya parameters.

Constitutional Links: Articles 5–11, Citizenship Act 1955 amendments.

4.1.1 Birth, Descent, Registration
→ Registration requirements: 7 years of ordinary residence prior to application

360° Coverage Boundaries

OUT OF SCOPE: Complex global dual-citizenship agreements between non-Indian nations.

Q1. Which Article of the Indian Constitution explicitly empowers the Parliament to regulate the right of citizenship by law?
  • A) Article 8
  • B) Article 9
  • C) Article 10
  • D) Article 11
Reveal Answer & Core Insights

Correct Answer: D

Reference: Article 11 of Part II.

Explanation: Article 11 grants complete omnibus power to Parliament to make provisions regarding acquisition, termination, and all other matters relating to citizenship. This power was utilized to pass the Citizenship Act, 1955.

Q2. An Indian citizen automatically loses their citizenship status through 'Termination' if they:
  • A) Voluntarily acquire the citizenship of another foreign country
  • B) Reside outside India continuously for a period of two years for commercial work
  • C) Trade unlawfully with an enemy state during a declared war
  • D) Register as an Overseas Citizen of India (OCI) cardholder
Reveal Answer & Core Insights

Correct Answer: A

Reference: Section 9 of the Citizenship Act, 1955.

Explanation: Under Termination, when an Indian citizen voluntarily acquires citizenship of another country, Indian citizenship automatically halts. This mirrors Article 9 which bars dual nationality.

[CRITICAL] War Exception Window The termination provision *does not apply* during a war in which India is actively engaged. The state holds the termination until geopolitical stability returns to prevent treasonous escape.
Q3. To acquire Indian citizenship by naturalization, an ordinary applicant must possess functional knowledge of a language specified in which schedule of the Constitution?
  • A) Seventh Schedule
  • B) Eighth Schedule
  • C) Ninth Schedule
  • D) Tenth Schedule
Reveal Answer & Core Insights

Correct Answer: B

Reference: Third Schedule of the Citizenship Act 1955 (Qualifications for Naturalisation).

Explanation: The applicant must possess adequate knowledge of at least one language listed in the Eighth Schedule of the Constitution (which currently features 22 constitutionally recognized languages).

Q1. Which of the following constitutional Writs can be issued against private individuals as well as public state authorities?
  • A) Mandamus
  • B) Certiorari
  • C) Habeas Corpus
  • D) Prohibition
Reveal Answer & Core Insights

Correct Answer: C

Reference: Article 32 Writs operational logic.

Explanation: Habeas Corpus ("To have the body of") is the only writ that can be addressed to a private citizen alongside a state official who has illegally detained someone. Mandamus, Certiorari, and Prohibition require a public, statutory, or judicial authority vector.

[GEM] Mandamus Limitations Mandamus *cannot* be issued against the President of India, State Governors, or Chief Justices of High Courts acting in a judicial capacity.
Q2. According to Article 13 of the Indian Constitution, any law that violates Fundamental Rights can be declared void. The term "Law" under Article 13 includes which of the following?
  • A) Only permanent acts passed by Parliament
  • B) Permanent acts, temporary ordinances, executive orders, notifications, and customs having the force of law
  • C) Only written statutory laws, excluding unwritten customs
  • D) Constitutional amendments passed under Article 368 exclusively
Reveal Answer & Core Insights

Correct Answer: B

Reference: Article 13(3) structural definitions.

Explanation: Article 13 provides an extensive definition of law: permanent statutes, temporary ordinances, delegated legislation (bye-laws, rules, regulations, statutory notifications), and non-statutory sources like customs or usage holding the force of law.

Q3. In the context of public employment, which clause of the Constitution directly enables the state to implement reservations for backward classes if they are under-represented in state services?
  • A) Article 16(2)
  • B) Article 16(4)
  • C) Article 15(3)
  • D) Article 14
Reveal Answer & Core Insights

Correct Answer: B

Reference: Article 16 (Equality of opportunity in public employment).

Explanation: Article 16(4) explicitly empowers the State to make provisions for the reservation of appointments or posts in favour of any backward class of citizens which, in the opinion of the State, is not adequately represented in the services under the State.

20. Consumer Protection Forums

Consumer Rights Architecture

The Consumer Protection Act 2019 replaced the older 1986 framework. It established the Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA) and set up a three-tier dispute resolution mechanism (District, State, and National commissions).

Q1. The Consumer Protection Act 2019 introduced which new apex regulatory body to prevent unfair trade practices and false advertisements?
  • A) Consumer Guidance Society
  • B) Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA)
  • C) National Consumer Arbitrator
  • D) Bureau of Indian Standards Control Board
Answer
Correct Answer: B
The CCPA was established under the 2019 Act to protect, promote, and enforce the rights of consumers as a class.

21. Human Rights Charters

Human Rights Institutional Framework

Key focus areas include the Protection of Human Rights Act 1993, the composition of the National Human Rights Commission (NHRC), and the State Human Rights Commission (SHRC). Amendments in 2019 shortened the chairperson's tenure from 5 years to 3 years.

Q1. Following the 2019 amendments to the Protection of Human Rights Act, the tenure of office for the Chairperson of the NHRC was reduced to:
  • A) 5 years or 70 years of age
  • B) 3 years or 70 years of age
  • C) 3 years or 65 years of age
  • D) 4 years or 68 years of age
Answer
Correct Answer: B
The 2019 amendment reduced the tenure of the NHRC/SHRC chairperson and members to 3 years (or up to 70 years of age, whichever comes first) to expedite leadership rotation.

22. Political Parties and Political System in India

Party System Recognition Parameters

India features a multi-party system. The Election Commission of India sets clear quantitative criteria for recognizing National and State parties based on vote shares and seat tallies in general elections.

Q1. To secure formal recognition as a "National Party" in India, a political party must satisfy which of the following criteria?
  • A) Win at least 2% of seats in Lok Sabha from at least 3 different states
  • B) Be recognized as a state party in at least four states
  • C) Secure 6% of valid votes in 4 states plus win 4 Lok Sabha seats
  • D) Any one of the above three conditions
Answer
Correct Answer: D
The ECI offers three distinct criteria for national party recognition; meeting any single condition satisfies the requirement.
