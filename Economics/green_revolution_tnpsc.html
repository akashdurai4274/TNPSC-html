    01Background & Historical Context

    ⚡ CRITICAL — Root Cause

    In 1960, India faced a severe food shortage. The country was importing wheat from other nations — a sign of extreme agricultural backwardness. Famines were widespread, and the production of food crops like rice and wheat was critically inadequate.

    Pre-Green Revolution India was characterised by traditional farming methods with no modern technology. The government realised that without a dramatic intervention, food insecurity would be permanent.

    ⚠ TRAP — Common Mistake

    Students often assume the Green Revolution was announced by that name from the beginning. It was NOT. The programme was first launched as a different scheme, and the name "Green Revolution" came LATER — in 1966.

    1959–1960
    Severe famine and food shortage in India. Wheat imported from foreign countries. Crisis triggers need for agricultural revolution.
    1960
    Intensive Agricultural Development Programme (IADP) — "Vazhi Nadatthum Thittam" (வழிநடத்தும் திட்டம்) — launched in 7 pilot districts only.
    1966
    The process proved successful. William Gaud used the term "Green Revolution" in a speech/article. The name was adopted officially and the programme extended to all of India.
    2006–2007
    Second Green Revolution launched due to resurgence of food insecurity. Target: increase food grain output from 214 million tonnes to 400 million tonnes by 2020. Agricultural growth rate target: 5% to 6%.

    02Key Scientists & Contributors

    ⚡ CRITICAL — Must Know All Four Names

    All four names below are examinable. Know EXACTLY what role each person played — they are frequently confused.

    CRITICAL
    Dr. M. S. Swaminathan
    🇮🇳 Father of Indian Green Revolution

    An agricultural scientist who conceptualised and implemented the Green Revolution in India. He adapted Norman Borlaug's global ideas to suit Indian conditions. Later served as a Rajya Sabha MP — nominated during President A.P.J. Abdul Kalam's tenure in 2002. During the second Green Revolution (2006–07), he was in Rajya Sabha, giving him additional influence.

    CRITICAL
    Norman Borlaug
    🌍 Father of World Green Revolution

    An American scientist who first developed the ideology of the Green Revolution globally. His concepts were studied and adapted by Swaminathan for India. It was Borlaug's framework that Swaminathan modified and applied with changes suited to Indian agriculture.

    PYQ
    William Gaud
    🗣️ Person who coined the term "Green Revolution"

    An American who, in a speech/article in 1967–68, used the phrase "Green Revolution" while describing the agricultural transformation. The term was then officially adopted. He did not implement the programme — he only named it.

    ⚠ TRAP

    William Gaud coined the term. He did NOT implement the Green Revolution. Do not confuse him with Swaminathan or Borlaug.

    KEY FIGURE
    C. Subramaniam
    🏛️ Agriculture Minister of India (at the time of Green Revolution)

    Served as the Agriculture Minister when the Green Revolution was being implemented. He provided the governmental and political support needed to execute Swaminathan's vision. Without his backing, the programme could not have been implemented at scale.

    💡 TIP — Memory Trick for the Four Names

    Borlaug = Big world idea → Swaminathan = Suited it to India → Gaud = Gave it the name → Subramaniam = State support (Minister)

    📋 PYQ — Frequently Asked

    "Father of Indian Green Revolution" = Dr. M. S. Swaminathan
    "Father of World Green Revolution" = Norman Borlaug
    "Who coined the term Green Revolution" = William Gaud

    03Government Schemes & Programmes

    KEY SCHEME

    Intensive Agricultural Development Programme (IADP)

    Tamil Name: வழிநடத்தும் திட்டம் (Vazhi Nadatthum Thittam — "Guiding/Pilot Programme")

    Year: 1960

    Scope: Only 7 districts (pilot/trial)

    • HYV seeds distributed to farmers in these 7 districts
    • Chemical fertilisers (nitrogen-based) provided
    • Irrigation facilities expanded
    • Modern equipment (tractors, pump sets) introduced
    • Pesticides provided

    Rationale for limiting to 7 districts: If the experiment failed across all of India, losses would be catastrophic. Limiting to 7 districts contained the risk.

    ⚠ TRAP — IADP ≠ Green Revolution

    The IADP (1960) was the programme. "Green Revolution" is the name given to the process/outcome in 1966. IADP was not renamed Green Revolution — the term Green Revolution was coined for the agricultural transformation process triggered by IADP.

    KEY SCHEME

    Second Green Revolution (இரண்டாவது பசுமை புரட்சி)

    Year: 2006–2007

    Context: Food insecurity resurfaced. India risked dependence on foreign countries again for food crops.

    • Target 1: Increase food grain production from 214 million tonnes → 400 million tonnes by 2020
    • Target 2: Raise agricultural growth rate from 5% → 6%

    M. S. Swaminathan was serving as Rajya Sabha MP during this period (nominated during Abdul Kalam's presidency), giving the movement expert legislative backing.

    04HYV Seeds — High Yielding Variety

    ⚡ CRITICAL — Core Concept

    HYV (High Yielding Variety) Seeds are the backbone of the Green Revolution. Understanding what they are and their limitations is essential.

    What are HYV Seeds?

    Two different varieties of the same crop (e.g., rice) are selected — each with a distinct superior quality (one may have high disease resistance; another high yield capacity). These two are cross-bred to produce a single hybrid seed that carries both qualities. This hybrid seed is called an HYV seed.

    CRITICAL

    Key Properties of HYV Seeds

    • High yield — significantly more output per area
    • Quick maturation — grows faster than normal seeds
    • Requires large amounts of water (4× more than normal seeds)
    • Requires chemical/nitrogen fertilisers to thrive
    • More susceptible to pest attacks than natural seeds
    • Cannot be saved and reused — seeds from HYV harvest do NOT produce the same quality in the next season

    Crops Where HYV Seeds Were Introduced

    • Rice (Arisi / நெல்) — Primary focus
    • Wheat (Kodumai / கோதுமை) — Primary focus
    • Maize / Corn (Makka Cholam / மக்காச் சோளம்) — Secondary focus
    ⚡ CRITICAL — What HYV Seeds were NOT used for

    HYV seeds were NOT developed for: Oil seeds (எண்ணெய் வித்துக்கள்), Pulses (பருப்பு வகைகள்), or other non-food crops. The entire focus was on food crops only — specifically rice and wheat.

    💎 GEM — Insight from Lecture

    The concept of HYV seeds came from Norman Borlaug as "Nutritive Seeds." Swaminathan studied Borlaug's concept and adapted it to Indian conditions with modifications. That's why Borlaug is "Father of World" and Swaminathan is "Father of India" — the latter made it work HERE.

    ⚠ TRAP — The Seed Reuse Problem

    A farmer who grows a crop from HYV seeds cannot take seeds from that harvest and replant them to get the same yield. The cross-bred advantage disappears in the second generation. Farmers had to keep buying new HYV seeds every season — this created economic dependence and was a major limitation of the Green Revolution.

    05Components of the Green Revolution

    COMPONENT 1

    HYV Seeds (அதிக மகசூல் விதைகள்)

    Cross-bred hybrid seeds introduced for rice, wheat, and maize. Provided high and rapid yield.

    COMPONENT 2

    Chemical Fertilisers — Nitrogen-Based (ரசாயன உரங்கள்)

    Synthetic nitrogen fertilisers applied to accelerate crop growth. Nitrogen causes rapid growth and higher yields. However, prolonged use degraded soil quality.

    COMPONENT 3

    Pesticides / Insecticides (பூச்சி கொல்லிகள்)

    Chemical pesticides used to protect HYV crops from pests (HYV seeds are more vulnerable to pest attacks than natural seeds).

    COMPONENT 4

    Irrigation & Water Supply (பாசன வசதி)

    HYV crops require 4× more water than traditional crops. Extensive canal irrigation systems and pump sets were deployed. This led to a significant fall in groundwater levels.

    COMPONENT 5

    Modern Machinery (நவீன இயந்திரங்கள்)

    Tractors, threshers (கதிரடிப்பான்கள்), pump sets and other modern agricultural equipment introduced. This boosted both speed and scale of farming. Manufacturing of these machines also increased, generating secondary economic activity.

    COMPONENT 6

    Livestock Rearing (கால்நடை வளர்ப்பு)

    Cattle, goats, and poultry rearing increased to support the agricultural ecosystem — for food, strength (draught animals), and utility.

    06Achievements of the Green Revolution

    Aspect Before Green Revolution After Green Revolution
    Wheat & Rice Production Low; India importing wheat Multiple-fold increase; self-sufficient
    Seed Technology Traditional local seeds HYV seeds — higher & faster yield
    Farm Machinery Manual / bullock-driven Tractors, threshers, pump sets
    Fertiliser Use Organic / natural manure Synthetic nitrogen fertilisers
    Rural Employment Limited agricultural jobs Increased village-level employment
    Food Security Famines; foreign dependence Self-reliance; no wheat imports needed
    Tractor Manufacturing Minimal Grew significantly to meet demand
    Food Production Target (2nd GR) 214 million tonnes (2006) Target: 400 million tonnes by 2020
    ⚡ CRITICAL — Key Achievement

    After the Green Revolution, India achieved food self-reliance — it no longer needed to import wheat from foreign countries. The production of rice and wheat increased manifold. Rural living standards improved due to higher farm income and employment.

    07Limitations & Criticism

    💎 GEM — Lecturer's Insight

    The Green Revolution was not without negatives. While it solved the immediate food crisis, it created long-term environmental and socio-economic damage. A complete answer in interviews must acknowledge BOTH its achievements AND its failures.

    CRITICAL

    1. Loss of Biodiversity (பல்லுயிர் இல்லாமை)

    Farmers grew only rice and wheat — a monoculture. Diverse crops like oil seeds, pulses, and other food varieties were neglected. This extreme dependence on single crops is considered a major ecological failure.

    CRITICAL

    2. Soil Degradation (மண் வளம் குறைதல்)

    Continuous planting of the same crop (no crop rotation) exhausted soil nutrients. Repeated use of chemical fertilisers raised soil alkalinity (pH levels), destroying soil micronutrients and microbial life. The soil "forgot" how to support other crops.

    💎 Live Gem — Lecturer's Analogy

    The lecturer compared soil degradation to a skilled person who hasn't practised a skill for 10 years — they lose the ability. "Touch விட்டுட்டா" (lost touch). Similarly, soil that only grew rice/wheat lost its capacity to support other crops.

    CRITICAL

    3. Groundwater Depletion (நிலத்தடி நீர்மட்டம் குறைதல்)

    HYV crops needed far more water than traditional crops. Farmers dug deep borewells and used pump sets aggressively, causing a severe fall in groundwater levels. This eventually led to water scarcity.

    LIMITATION

    4. HYV Seeds Cannot Be Reused (விதை மலட்டுத்தன்மை)

    Seeds saved from HYV harvests do NOT produce the same high yield in the next season. Farmers had to buy new seeds every season from the government/suppliers, creating economic dependence and added cost.

    LIMITATION

    5. Health Impact from Chemicals (உணவின் மீதான தாக்கம்)

    Crops grown with heavy use of pesticides and synthetic fertilisers were found to have negative impacts on human health. Before the Green Revolution, only organic/natural manure was used.

    LIMITATION

    6. Income Inequality (வருமான ஏற்றத்தாழ்வு)

    HYV seeds, chemical fertilisers, and modern machinery were expensive. Wealthy farmers could afford them easily; poor farmers could not. This widened the economic gap between rich and poor farmers.

    LIMITATION

    7. Increased Pest Vulnerability

    HYV crops are more susceptible to pest attacks than natural seed varieties. This meant more pesticides were needed, creating a cycle of chemical use and associated environmental damage.

    LIMITATION

    8. No Crop Rotation (பயிர் சுழற்சி இல்லாமை)

    Farmers planted only rice or only wheat repeatedly on the same land without rotating with other crops. This depleted soil nutrients as the soil lacked the diverse inputs from varied crops.

    LIMITATION

    9. Lack of Farmer Awareness

    Many farmers lacked proper guidance on how to correctly use chemical fertilisers and pesticides. Improper application led to additional environmental and yield problems.

    09Definition & Key Terms

    CRITICAL

    Official Definition — Green Revolution

    "The period during which new technologies were used to increase agricultural production worldwide is called the Green Revolution."

    In India specifically: the use of HYV seeds, chemical fertilisers, pesticides, irrigation, and modern machinery to dramatically increase the production of rice and wheat.

    HYV Seeds
    High Yielding Variety seeds — hybrid seeds created by cross-breeding two varieties with distinct superior qualities to combine both in one seed.
    IADP
    Intensive Agricultural Development Programme — the pilot scheme launched in 1960 in 7 districts that formed the foundation of the Green Revolution.
    Monoculture
    Growing the same crop repeatedly on the same land without rotation — a criticism of Green Revolution agriculture.
    Crop Rotation
    Alternating different types of crops on the same land across seasons to maintain soil nutrients — this was largely absent during the Green Revolution.
    Nitrogen Fertiliser
    Synthetic fertiliser rich in nitrogen that accelerates crop growth. Overuse leads to increased soil alkalinity and degradation.
    Groundwater Depletion
    Reduction of underground water levels due to excessive extraction via borewells and pump sets to meet HYV irrigation needs.
    Biodiversity Loss
    Reduction in the variety of species cultivated; in the context of GR, the neglect of pulses, oil seeds, and other diverse food crops.
    Cross-Breeding
    The process of combining two different plant varieties to produce offspring with the desirable traits of both parents — the mechanism behind HYV seed creation.

    10💡 Analogy — Understanding the Green Revolution

    💡 Analogy: Upgrading from an Old Feature Phone to a Smartphone

    Imagine India's agriculture in 1960 as someone using a very old, basic feature phone. It barely makes calls. Then the Green Revolution happened — like upgrading to a smartphone. Here's how every part maps:

    India's 1960 food crisis
    →
    Your old phone dying — can't even make calls anymore. You're borrowing someone else's phone (importing wheat from foreign countries).
    Traditional seeds
    →
    Old phone battery — works, but slowly, barely holds charge.
    HYV Seeds
    →
    New high-performance battery — charges fast, lasts long, handles heavy usage. But if you try to copy it into another phone, it doesn't work the same (seeds can't be reused).
    Chemical fertilisers (nitrogen)
    →
    Turbo-charger or performance booster app — makes everything faster. But overuse drains the device long-term (soil degradation).
    Pesticides
    →
    Antivirus software — protects from threats. But powerful antivirus also deletes good files (kills beneficial microorganisms in soil).
    Tractors & modern machinery
    →
    Upgrading from manually typing to using voice commands and AI — everything is faster and more efficient.
    IADP — 7 pilot districts
    →
    Beta testing the new phone OS on 7 selected users before rolling out to all 1.4 billion people.
    1966 — Official Green Revolution launch
    →
    Official public release of the new OS — now everyone gets the upgrade.
    Monoculture (only rice/wheat)
    →
    Only ever running one app (WhatsApp) — all phone resources dedicated to it. Other apps (skills) atrophy.
    Groundwater depletion
    →
    Leaving your new smartphone on max brightness and 5G constantly — great performance short-term, but battery health deteriorates permanently.
    Second Green Revolution (2006–07)
    →
    Your upgraded phone started slowing down after years of heavy use — so you do a major OS update (v2.0) to restore performance.
    Income inequality in GR
    →
    New smartphones are expensive — rich people get the best model; poor people stuck with budget versions or can't afford them at all.

    11Expected Exam Questions

    Q →
    Who is called the Father of the Indian Green Revolution?
    ⚡ CRITICAL 📋 PYQ

    Dr. M. S. Swaminathan is called the Father of the Indian Green Revolution. He was an agricultural scientist who introduced and adapted the Green Revolution concept for India, building on Norman Borlaug's global framework.

    Additional facts: He also served as a Rajya Sabha MP, nominated during President A.P.J. Abdul Kalam's tenure in 2002.

    💎 Live Gem

    There are TWO fathers to remember: Norman Borlaug (World) and M.S. Swaminathan (India). The distinction: Borlaug invented the ideology globally; Swaminathan adapted and executed it specifically for India.

    Q →
    Who coined the term "Green Revolution"?
    ⚡ CRITICAL ⚠ TRAP

    William Gaud (an American) coined the term "Green Revolution" in 1967–68 in an article/speech. He used this phrase to describe the agricultural transformation that had already taken place. The name was then officially adopted in 1966.

    💎 Live Gem — TRAP Warning

    William Gaud did NOT implement the Green Revolution. He only NAMED it. Don't confuse him with Swaminathan (implementer) or Borlaug (originator of the concept). This distinction is a classic exam trap.

    Q →
    What is the IADP? What was its purpose?
    📋 PYQ

    IADP = Intensive Agricultural Development Programme (Tamil: வழிநடத்தும் திட்டம் — Guiding/Pilot Programme)

    Launched in 1960 in 7 pilot districts of India. It was the first step of what later became known as the Green Revolution. HYV seeds, chemical fertilisers, irrigation support, and modern machinery were provided exclusively to these 7 districts as a trial.

    The rationale for limiting to 7 districts: if the experiment failed, losses would be contained; if successful, it could be scaled nationally.

    Q →
    What are HYV seeds? What are their limitations?
    ⚡ CRITICAL

    HYV (High Yielding Variety) seeds are hybrid seeds created by cross-breeding two varieties of the same crop, each with a distinct superior trait, to produce a single seed carrying both qualities.

    Benefits: High yield, rapid growth, increased food production.

    Limitations:

    • Require 4× more water than traditional seeds
    • Seeds from HYV harvests cannot be reused — farmers must buy new seeds every season
    • More susceptible to pest attacks
    • Were developed only for rice, wheat, and maize — NOT for oil seeds or pulses
    Q →
    What were the main criticisms / limitations of the Green Revolution?
    ⚡ CRITICAL
    • Loss of biodiversity — monoculture; only rice & wheat grown
    • Soil degradation — no crop rotation, chemical overuse, increased soil pH
    • Groundwater depletion — excessive irrigation lowered water tables
    • HYV seed sterility — seeds can't be reused; dependency on annual purchase
    • Health risks — food grown with heavy chemicals impacts human health
    • Income inequality — only wealthy farmers could afford the inputs
    • Increased pest vulnerability — HYV crops attacked more easily
    • Neglected crops — oil seeds and pulses were entirely ignored
    Q →
    What is the Second Green Revolution? When was it launched and what were its targets?
    📋 PYQ

    The Second Green Revolution was launched in 2006–2007 by the Indian government to address resurging food insecurity and dependence on foreign nations for food crops.

    Targets set:

    • Increase food grain production from 214 million tonnes to 400 million tonnes by 2020
    • Raise the agricultural growth rate from 5% to 6%

    Dr. M. S. Swaminathan was serving as Rajya Sabha MP during this period, providing expert legislative support to the second wave.

    Q →
    Why was the Green Revolution needed in 1960?
    💎 GEM

    In the late 1950s and 1960, India faced a severe food shortage. Rice and wheat production was critically low. India was importing wheat from foreign countries — a sign of extreme vulnerability. Famines were a real and present danger. There was no modern technology in agriculture.

    Dr. M. S. Swaminathan, then an agricultural scientist, recommended a structured approach to the government. His solution — the IADP — was piloted in 7 districts, proved successful, and was scaled nationally as the Green Revolution.

    💎 Live Gem — Interview Insight

    In an interview or Group-1 exam, connect the background story: "What if the Green Revolution had NOT happened?" Answer: India would have remained permanently dependent on foreign wheat imports, famines would have continued, and agricultural backwardness would have stunted India's entire economic growth path. The Green Revolution wasn't just about food — it was about national sovereignty and self-reliance.

    Q →
    Who was the Agriculture Minister who supported the Green Revolution's implementation?
    ⚡ CRITICAL

    C. Subramaniam was the Agriculture Minister of India when the Green Revolution was being implemented. He provided government backing for Swaminathan's scientific vision, enabling the programme to be executed administratively.

    The successful implementation of the Green Revolution in India is attributed to BOTH: Dr. M. S. Swaminathan (scientific vision) and C. Subramaniam (administrative & political support).
