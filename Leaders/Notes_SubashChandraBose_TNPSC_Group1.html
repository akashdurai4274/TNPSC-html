Early Life & Background

Subash Chandra Bose was born on 23 January in Cuttack, which is part of present-day Odisha. His father was Janakinath Bose — "Bose" (also spelled "Bosh" in some references) is the family's community/social surname. His mother was Prabhavati Devi.

From a very young age, Bose was deeply drawn to the ideas of Swami Vivekananda, whom he accepted as his spiritual guru. This spiritual foundation shaped his fearless, idealistic personality throughout his life.

Even during his school years, Bose developed friction with British authorities. His father, seeing that staying in India would lead to constant confrontation, arranged to send him abroad. In 1919, Bose left for London, England.

His birthday, 23 January, is now celebrated as Parakram Divas (Parakram = Bravery/Valour) — a national day on which India also awards the Disaster Management Award. Individuals receive ₹5 lakh and organisations receive ₹51 lakh (approximately 5 million rupees) for distinguished work in disaster management. This award is given on Parakram Divas in his memory.

ICS Posting & Historic Resignation

After securing the 4th rank in the ICS exam in 1920, Bose received his posting. However, he was witnessing the unfolding of the Jallianwala Bagh massacre and the rising Non-Cooperation Movement. These events made him realise he could not serve two masters — the British Empire and the Indian people — simultaneously.

In April 1921, he resigned from the ICS posting. He could not serve under British rule while also fighting for India's freedom. As the lecturer notes, giving up such a prestigious, high-paying post (equivalent to an IAS officer today) to join the freedom struggle was an extraordinary sacrifice.

After resigning, he directly went to meet Mahatma Gandhi. Gandhi told him: "You are from West Bengal; there is a great leader there — follow him and work under him." That leader was C.R. Das (Chittaranjan Das) of Calcutta — a key figure in West Bengal politics and co-founder (with Motilal Nehru) of the Swaraj Party. Bose accepted C.R. Das as his political guru.

Bose joined the Indian National Congress in 1921.

Critical
Bose resigned from his ICS posting in April 1921. He joined the Indian National Congress in 1921. He met Gandhi who directed him to C.R. Das. C.R. Das became his political guru.

Political Career & Congress Years

After joining Congress in 1921, Bose became an increasingly prominent and radical voice. The British, alarmed by his activities in West Bengal, imposed an Emergency Act on the Bengal province in 1924 and imprisoned him in Alipore Jail (also known as Calcutta Central Jail).

Within Congress, Bose was firmly in the extremist (tīvira tēciyavādi) camp — alongside figures like Bhagat Singh in the North, Vasudevan and Muthuramalingam Thevar in Tamil Nadu. He opposed the moderate faction and consistently pushed for complete independence.

He actively opposed two important positions: (1) the Dominion Status demand put forward by his own political guru C.R. Das and Motilal Nehru — he believed India must have full independence, not partial self-governance under British defence and foreign policy; and (2) the withdrawal of the Civil Disobedience Movement by Gandhi after the Gandhi-Irwin Pact and Gandhi's participation in the Second Round Table Conference.

In 1938 and 1939, Bose presided over two consecutive Congress sessions — Haripura (1938) and Tripura (1939). At Tripura, he stood against Pattabhi Sitaramayya, who was Gandhi's preferred candidate. Bose won the election — but Gandhi's displeasure was clear, calling it his own "defeat." Out of respect for Gandhi (whom Bose acknowledged as the Nation's Father despite their ideological differences), Bose resigned from Congress in 1939.

After leaving Congress, Bose visited Madurai in Tamil Nadu and met Muthuramalingam Thevar. Together, they co-founded the Forward Bloc party in 1939. The Forward Bloc spread across India but is most prominent in West Bengal, Jharkhand, and Tamil Nadu, where the party still exists today.

In 1940–41, the British, unable to imprison him without public backlash, placed Bose under house arrest.

The Great Escape (1941)

While under house arrest in Calcutta in 1940–41, Bose hatched a daring escape plan. He confided in his nephew (his elder brother's son), who visited him regularly. Bose planned to escape through Afghanistan (then part of British India's border territory, now Pakistan–Afghanistan border) where his friend Bhagat Ram Talwar was waiting.

To disguise himself, Bose shaved clean and then wore a fake beard and moustache to look like a Muslim man. He dressed in a large Afghan Muslim robe (a colourful full-length garment typical of foreign Muslims) and wore a turban. He adopted the false identity of Rahmat Khan (sometimes rendered as "Ziauddin" in other accounts; the transcript specifically gives Rahmat Khan).

In this disguise, he escaped from his Calcutta home, travelled through Afghanistan, and made his way to Germany — where Hitler was in power and the Second World War was escalating.

Gem
Bose's disguise identity during the Great Escape: Rahmat Khan — dressed as an Afghan Muslim traveller with a fake beard, robe, and turban. His contact in Afghanistan was Bhagat Ram Talwar.

INA, Azad Hind & Singapore

After reaching Germany, Bose met Hitler and sought support. However, Hitler's book The Führer (German: "Der Führer" — meaning "The Leader") had reportedly described Indians in an unflattering way. Hitler gave him limited support and suggested he seek help from Japan.

In 1942, Bose travelled to Burma (Myanmar), where Japanese forces had taken control from the British. Japan was fighting on the Axis side (Germany, Italy, Japan) against the Allied forces (Britain, France). Bose gathered Indian prisoners of war and political exiles deported there by the British, and began forming a military unit.

In 1943, Bose moved to Singapore. There he found two key figures who had already established a military formation: Mohan Singh and Rash Behari Bose (Ras Bihari Ghosh). They had founded the Indian National Army (INA). They handed over command of the INA to Netaji Subash Chandra Bose.

The INA's original name was Azad Hind Fauj — "Azad" meaning free/liberated, "Hind" meaning India, "Fauj" meaning army. So: Free India Army. In Singapore, Bose also established a Provisional Government of Free India (Azad Hind Government / Indian Independence League), declaring India's liberation.

From Singapore, Bose made radio broadcasts declaring: "Chalo Delhi!" — "March towards Delhi! Let us liberate the nation!"

Within the INA, Bose established a special Women's Regiment (பெண்கள் படைப்பிரிவு). He appointed Lakshmi Sahgal — a doctor from Tamil Nadu who had gone to Singapore to study medicine — as its commander. This appointment is historically significant.

Critical
INA (Indian National Army) was originally founded by Mohan Singh and Rash Behari Bose. Netaji took over command from them in 1943 in Singapore. Do NOT say Netaji founded the INA — he took command of it.
Trap
The INA was NOT founded by Subash Chandra Bose. It was founded by Mohan Singh and Rash Behari Bose (Ghosh). Netaji took over as Commander. This distinction is specifically tested in exams.

In 2002, when Abdul Kalam was elected President (BJP nomination by Vajpayee), Congress nominated Lakshmi Sahgal as their Presidential candidate. Two big connections in one person!

The Full Story of "Jai Hind"

The famous slogan Jai Hind did not originate with Netaji — it has a fascinating three-person origin story.

Step 1: In 1933, a Tamil man named Chembakaramam Pillai (சேம்பகராமன் பிள்ளை) spoke at a conference in Vienna, Austria. He said: "Jai Hindustan Ke!" Chembakaramam Pillai had a close friendship with both Hitler and Egyptian leader Nasser. His slogan was the first use of this kind of nationalist chant. (This name was in older syllabuses but has been removed from current ones.)

Step 2: Netaji, inspired by Chembakaramam Pillai's phrase, adopted "Jai Hindustan" as the INA's battle cry.

Step 3: A friend of Netaji from Hyderabad, Abdul Hassan (a Muslim, studying engineering in Germany), raised an objection: "Hindustan sounds like a country only for Hindus — Muslim soldiers may not want to join the INA under that name." He suggested replacing "Hindustan" with "Hind" — which simply means India, without any religious connotation. Netaji agreed, and "Jai Hind" became the official slogan — adopted nationwide.

Critical
Two different exam questions, two different answers: (1) "Who first gave the slogan Jai Hind?" → Chembakaramam Pillai (Tamil man, Vienna, 1933) (2) "Who spread / popularised Jai Hind nationwide?" → Netaji Subash Chandra Bose

Death & Controversy

In 1945, as World War II ended — Hitler died by suicide, Japan surrendered after the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki by America — the Axis powers collapsed. Bose was in Bangkok (Thailand's capital), under Japanese protection. With Japan's surrender, British forces were moving to recapture the region.

Bose decided to fly to Tokyo, Japan. On the way, his flight passed over Taiwan — then known as Formosa (a Portuguese word meaning "beautiful island"). A plane crash occurred over or on Taiwan, and it is reported that Bose died from his injuries. However, his death has never been conclusively proven.

Because the circumstances were unclear, the Indian government set up three committees headed by judges to investigate whether he actually died or escaped. One of these committees was asked about specifically in a Group 2 exam.

PYQ
Three committees were formed to investigate Bose's death. One of these committees was asked in a Group 2 exam. Remember: his death is disputed — some say he died in the Taiwan plane crash, others say he survived and lived elsewhere.
Gem
Taiwan = Formosa (Portuguese: "Beautiful Island"). The Portuguese were the first to discover this island, just as Vasco da Gama discovered India. They named it Formosa. This is where the plane crash allegedly occurred in 1945.
