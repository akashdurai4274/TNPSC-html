01 THE PREAMBLE

WE, THE PEOPLE OF INDIA, having solemnly resolved to constitute India into a SOVEREIGN SOCIALIST SECULAR DEMOCRATIC REPUBLIC and to secure to all its citizens:

JUSTICE, social, economic and political;
LIBERTY of thought, expression, belief, faith and worship;
EQUALITY of status and of opportunity; and to promote among them all
FRATERNITY assuring the dignity of the individual and the unity and integrity of the Nation;

IN OUR CONSTITUENT ASSEMBLY this twenty-sixth day of November, 1949, do HEREBY ADOPT, ENACT AND GIVE TO OURSELVES THIS CONSTITUTION.

Word-by-Word Breakdown

KEYWORD MEANING / SIGNIFICANCE ADDED BY
Sovereign India is independent; not under any foreign power. Final authority rests with the people. Original (1949)
Socialist 🔥 Commitment to social and economic equality; no extreme concentration of wealth. 42nd Amendment (1976)
Secular 🔥 State has no official religion; equal respect and protection for all religions. 42nd Amendment (1976)
Democratic Government elected by the people; adult franchise. Original (1949)
Republic Elected Head of State (President), not hereditary monarchy. Original (1949)
Justice Social, economic & political; borrowed from Russian Revolution ideals. Original (1949)
Liberty 5 aspects: thought, expression, belief, faith, worship. Original (1949)
Equality Equal status and opportunity for all citizens. Original (1949)
Fraternity Brotherhood among citizens; ensures dignity and national unity. Original (1949)
Integrity 🔥 Unity and integrity of the nation added to emphasize national cohesion. 42nd Amendment (1976)
🔴 CRITICAL
The Preamble was amended ONLY ONCE — by the 42nd Amendment Act, 1976 (during Emergency). Three words were inserted: Socialist, Secular, Integrity. The Preamble is NOT enforceable in court but is a key interpretive tool.
🟡 TRAP
The Preamble is NOT a source of power nor a limitation on legislative power. In Kesavananda Bharati (1973), the Supreme Court held that the Preamble is part of the Constitution and can be amended but its basic structure cannot be destroyed.

01 The Union and its Territory (Art. 1–4)

The Union and its Territory (Art. 1–4)

ARTICLE PROVISION KEY NOTES
1 🔥 Name and Territory of the Union India, that is Bharat, shall be a Union of States. Three categories of territory: States, UTs, and territories acquired. Note: "Union of States" not "Federation of States" — deliberate choice by Ambedkar.
2 Admission or Establishment of New States Parliament may by law admit new States or establish new States on such terms as it thinks fit. Example: Sikkim admitted as 22nd state via 36th Amendment (1975).
[2A] Sikkim to be associated with the Union — Omitted Inserted by 35th Amendment (1974), omitted by 36th Amendment (1975) when Sikkim became full state.
3 🔥 Formation of New States and Alteration of Areas, Boundaries or Names Parliament can: (a) form new state by separation/union (b) increase/diminish area of state (c) alter boundaries (d) alter name. Bill requires President's recommendation + State Legislature's opinion (not consent). President can override state opinion. Example: Telangana (2014) carved from Andhra Pradesh.
4 Laws under Art. 2 and 3 to Amend First and Fourth Schedules Laws made under Art. 2 and Art. 3 shall contain provisions for amending the First Schedule (States & UTs list) and Fourth Schedule (Rajya Sabha seat allocation). Such laws are NOT constitutional amendments under Art. 368 — only simple majority needed.
🔴 CRITICAL
Under Art. 3, Parliament can create, merge, rename, or alter boundaries of states by a simple majority — it is NOT a constitutional amendment. The concerned State Legislature's opinion must be sought but need not be accepted. This shows India's Union is indestructible — states can be created or destroyed but the Union cannot be. Contrast with USA where states cannot be divided without their consent.
🟡 TRAP
Art. 3 bills require President's prior recommendation before introduction in Parliament — NOT Speaker's permission. The state legislature's view is only consultative. Parliament is not bound by it. Also, newly created states get representation automatically through amendment of 1st and 4th Schedules under Art. 4 — no separate constitutional amendment needed.

02 Citizenship (Art. 5–11)

ARTICLE PROVISION KEY NOTES
5 🔥 Citizenship at the Commencement of the Constitution A person is a citizen at commencement (26 Jan 1950) if: (a) born in India, OR (b) either parent born in India, OR (c) ordinarily resident in India for 5 years preceding commencement.
6 Rights of Citizenship of Persons who Migrated from Pakistan Persons who migrated from Pakistan to India before 19 July 1948 — citizen if they or their parents/grandparents were born in undivided India. After 19 July 1948 — must have registered with prescribed authority after 6 months residence.
7 Rights of Citizenship of Certain Migrants to Pakistan Persons who migrated to Pakistan after 1 March 1947 shall NOT be citizens. Exception: those who returned under a permit for resettlement or permanent return can apply for citizenship.
8 Rights of Citizenship of Persons of Indian Origin Residing Outside India Person (or either parent/grandparent born in undivided India) residing outside India can be registered as citizen by diplomatic/consular representative of India. Basis for OCI (Overseas Citizen of India) concept.
9 Persons Voluntarily Acquiring Citizenship of a Foreign State Not to be Citizens If a person voluntarily acquires citizenship of any foreign country, they automatically cease to be an Indian citizen. India does NOT allow dual citizenship (unlike USA, UK). OCI card is NOT dual citizenship.
10 Continuance of the Rights of Citizenship Every person who is or deemed to be a citizen under Art. 5–8 shall continue to be a citizen subject to any law made by Parliament. Parliament can provide for termination of citizenship.
11 🔥 Parliament to Regulate the Right of Citizenship by Law Parliament has the exclusive power to make provisions with respect to the acquisition and termination of citizenship. Led to the Citizenship Act, 1955 — the substantive law governing citizenship in India today. Also basis for the Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2019 (CAA).
🔴 CRITICAL
India follows Single Citizenship (borrowed from UK) — unlike USA where citizens have both national and state citizenship. Art. 5–11 deal only with citizenship at the commencement of the Constitution (26 Jan 1950). For all subsequent citizenship matters, Citizenship Act, 1955 (enacted under Art. 11) applies. The Act provides for citizenship by: Birth, Descent, Registration, Naturalisation, and Incorporation of Territory.
🟡 TRAP
OCI (Overseas Citizen of India) is NOT dual citizenship — it is a long-term visa with certain rights but OCI holders cannot vote, hold constitutional office, or work in government. PIO (Person of Indian Origin) card scheme was merged with OCI in 2015. Under Art. 9, voluntary acquisition of foreign citizenship automatically terminates Indian citizenship — even without any formal declaration. The CAA 2019 (Citizenship Amendment Act) grants expedited citizenship to Hindu, Sikh, Buddhist, Jain, Parsi, and Christian minorities from Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan who entered India before 31 Dec 2014 — but NOT to Muslims from those countries.
Art. 5 to 11 — "BORN CAME LEFT OVERSEAS — CONTINUE PARLIAMENT"
5Born / Domicile in India at commencement
6Came from Pakistan (migrated to India)
7Left to Pakistan (migrated out — not citizen)
8Overseas Indians of Indian origin

03 FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS (Art. 12–35)

🟢 TIP
Originally 7 Fundamental Rights. Right to Property (Art. 31) was removed as a Fundamental Right by the 44th Amendment (1978) and made a legal right under Art. 300A. Now only 6 Fundamental Rights remain.

Right to Equality (Art. 14–18)

ARTICLE RIGHT KEY NOTES
14 🔥 Equality before law AND equal protection of laws Two concepts from English & American law. "Reasonable classification" is permitted.
15 Prohibition of discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, place of birth Art. 15(3) allows special provisions for women & children. Art. 15(4) & 15(5) for socially/educationally backward classes.
16 Equality of opportunity in public employment Art. 16(4) allows reservations for backward classes. Art. 16(4A) — SC/ST promotion reservation.
17 🔥 Abolition of Untouchability Untouchability abolished and its practice is a punishable offence. Art. 17 is enforceable against private individuals too.
18 Abolition of Titles No title except military/academic. State shall not confer titles. Bharat Ratna etc. are NOT "titles" — clarified by SC.

Right to Freedom (Art. 19–22)

ARTICLE RIGHT KEY NOTES
19 🔥 6 Freedoms: (a) Speech & Expression (b) Assembly (c) Association (d) Movement (e) Residence (f) Profession Originally 7; Art. 19(1)(f) — Property was deleted by 44th Amendment. All subject to reasonable restrictions.
20 Protection in respect of conviction for offences No ex-post facto law, no double jeopardy, no self-incrimination. Cannot be suspended even during Emergency.
21 🔥 Protection of life and personal liberty "Heart of the Constitution." Extended by Maneka Gandhi case (1978) — includes right to live with dignity, education, health, privacy etc.
21A Right to Education (6–14 years) Added by 86th Amendment (2002). Free and compulsory education.
22 Protection against arrest and detention Right to be informed of grounds, consult lawyer, produced before magistrate within 24 hours. MISA, COFEPOSA, NSA are preventive detention laws.

Right against Exploitation (Art. 23–24)

Art. 23Prohibition of traffic in human beings and forced labour (begar). Enforceable against private individuals too.
Art. 24Prohibition of employment of children below 14 years in factories, mines or hazardous employment.

Right to Freedom of Religion (Art. 25–28)

Art. 25Freedom of conscience and free profession, practice and propagation of religion. Subject to public order, morality, health.
Art. 26Freedom to manage religious affairs. Right to establish & maintain institutions for religious & charitable purposes.
Art. 27No person shall be compelled to pay taxes for promotion of any particular religion.
Art. 28No religious instruction in State-funded educational institutions. Students in aided institutions cannot be compelled to attend religious instruction.

Cultural and Educational Rights (Art. 29–30)

Art. 29Right of any section to conserve its language, script, culture. No denial of admission on grounds of religion, race, caste, language.
Art. 30Right of minorities (religious or linguistic) to establish and administer educational institutions. State shall not discriminate in granting aid.

Right to Constitutional Remedies (Art. 32–35)

Art. 32 🔥Right to move Supreme Court for enforcement of Fundamental Rights. Dr. Ambedkar called it "the heart and soul of the Constitution." Cannot be suspended except during Emergency under Art. 359.
Art. 33Parliament may modify FRs for armed forces, police, intelligence.
Art. 35Parliament (not state) has exclusive power to legislate on FRs under Arts. 16(3), 17, 23, 24.

Five Writs under Art. 32 & Art. 226

WRIT MEANING PURPOSE AGAINST WHOM
Habeas Corpus 🔥 "You shall have the body" To release a person from illegal detention Any authority or private person
Mandamus 🔥 "We command" To direct a public authority to perform its legal duty Public authority, NOT against President/Governor
Prohibition "To forbid" To prevent inferior court from exceeding jurisdiction Judicial/quasi-judicial bodies only
Certiorari "To be certified" To quash order of inferior court/tribunal Judicial/quasi-judicial bodies
Quo Warranto "By what authority" To challenge unlawful occupation of a public office Persons holding public office
🔴 CRITICAL — ART. 13
Art. 13 declares any pre-constitutional law and post-constitutional law that is inconsistent with or takes away/abridges Fundamental Rights to be void. It establishes the doctrine of judicial review. "State" includes Government, Parliament, Legislature of States, and all local or other authorities within India.
💎 LIVE GEM — THE EVOLUTION OF ART. 21
In A.K. Gopalan v. State of Madras (1950), SC held Art. 21 had a narrow meaning — procedure of any law, even harsh. But in Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978), SC overruled Gopalan and held that procedure must be just, fair and reasonable (importing due process). Since then, Art. 21 has been expanded to include: right to privacy, right to education, right to livelihood, right to a clean environment, right to speedy trial, right to die with dignity, and over 30 other rights. This is why Art. 21 is called the "expanding universe" of the Constitution.

🟡 TRAP — FRs vs DPSPs CONFLICT
Originally, FRs prevailed over DPSPs. But after Kesavananda Bharati (1973) and the 42nd Amendment, Parliament added Art. 31C to protect laws implementing Arts. 39(b) and (c). Later in Minerva Mills (1980), SC held that harmony between FRs and DPSPs is part of the Basic Structure. Neither can completely abrogate the other.

06 UNION EXECUTIVE (Art. 52–78)

The President (Art. 52–62)

ARTICLE PROVISION
52 There shall be a President of India
54 🔥 Election by Electoral College — elected members of both Houses + elected members of State Legislative Assemblies (+ UT assemblies after 70th Amendment)
55 Manner of election — proportional representation, single transferable vote
56 Term of office: 5 years. Can resign to Vice-President.
61 🔥 Impeachment: for violation of the Constitution — by both Houses with 2/3 majority of total membership. No judicial immunity during impeachment.
72 Pardoning powers: Pardon, Reprieve, Respite, Remission, Commutation. In cases of death sentence, court martial, union executive offences.
74 Council of Ministers headed by PM to aid and advise the President. President shall act on such advice. (44th Amend — "shall" replaced "may")
78 Duties of Prime Minister — to communicate to the President all decisions of Council of Ministers, inform President of matters relating to administration.
🔴 CRITICAL — PRESIDENT'S VETO POWER
The President has THREE types of veto:
  1. Absolute Veto — Withhold assent to a bill (used on advice of Cabinet or for private member bills)
  2. Suspensive Veto — Return bill for reconsideration (cannot do for Money Bills); must assent if re-passed
  3. Pocket Veto — Takes no action, keeps the bill pending indefinitely. India has this power unlike USA where 10-day limit applies.

Vice-President (Art. 63–71)

Art. 63 🔥There shall be a Vice-President of India. Ex-officio Chairman of Rajya Sabha.
Art. 66Election: by members of both Houses of Parliament in joint sitting. Elected by proportional representation with single transferable vote.
Art. 67Term: 5 years. Can resign to President. Removal by Rajya Sabha by majority and agreed by Lok Sabha.

Prime Minister & Council of Ministers (Art. 74–75)

Art. 75PM appointed by President. Other Ministers appointed on advice of PM. Council of Ministers collectively responsible to Lok Sabha (Art. 75(3)).

🟡 TRAP — RAJYA SABHA SPECIAL POWERS
Rajya Sabha is a permanent body. It has two exclusive powers: (1) Art. 249 — can by 2/3 majority empower Parliament to legislate on a State List subject for national interest. (2) Art. 312 — can by 2/3 majority create new All India Services.

🔴 CRITICAL — ART. 356 (PRESIDENT'S RULE)
Under Art. 356, President's Rule can be imposed if the constitutional machinery of a state has failed. After SR Bommai v. Union of India (1994), the SC held: (1) Imposition subject to judicial review; (2) Floor test must be conducted before dismissal of government; (3) Proclamation must be ratified by Parliament within 2 months (prior to SR Bommai it was not justiciable).

🔴 CRITICAL — ART. 368: AMENDMENT PROCEDURE
Three types of amendment procedures:
  1. Simple Majority — Ordinary laws (not under Art. 368); e.g., creating new states (Art. 3), admission of new states
  2. Special Majority — By 2/3 majority of members present & voting AND majority of total membership of each House
  3. Special Majority + State Ratification — For provisions relating to federal structure (e.g., Art. 54, 55, 73, 162, 241, Chapter IV of Part V, Chapter V of Part VI, any list of 7th Schedule, representation in Parliament, Art. 368 itself)
No joint sitting for constitutional amendment bills. SC cannot be enlarged by amendment.

11 LANDMARK CASES

KESAVANANDA BHARATI v. STATE OF KERALA | 1973 | 13-judge bench
Established the Basic Structure Doctrine — Parliament can amend any provision of the Constitution but cannot destroy its basic structure. Basic structure includes: supremacy of Constitution, secular character, democratic republic, federal character, separation of powers, rule of law, judicial review, free and fair elections, individual freedoms. Overruled I.C. Golak Nath case. Most important constitutional case ever decided.
GOLAKNATH v. STATE OF PUNJAB | 1967 | SC
Held that Parliament cannot amend Fundamental Rights. Overruled by Kesavananda Bharati (1973) after 24th Constitutional Amendment.
MANEKA GANDHI v. UNION OF INDIA | 1978 | SC
Expanded Art. 21 — procedure affecting personal liberty must be just, fair and reasonable (due process). Established inter-connection between Art. 14, 19, and 21. Overruled narrow A.K. Gopalan interpretation.
MINERVA MILLS v. UNION OF INDIA | 1980 | SC
Struck down the 42nd Amendment provisions making Art. 31C absolute. Held that harmony between Fundamental Rights and DPSP is part of the Basic Structure. Parliament cannot give absolute primacy to DPSPs over FRs.
SR BOMMAI v. UNION OF INDIA | 1994 | 9-judge bench
Federalism is part of Basic Structure. Imposition of President's Rule under Art. 356 is subject to judicial review. Floor test must be conducted before dismissing a state government. Proclamation must be approved by Parliament within 2 months.
INDRA SAWHNEY v. UNION OF INDIA (MANDAL CASE) | 1992 | 9-judge bench
Upheld 27% OBC reservation but struck down 10% EWS reservation (at that time). Laid down 50% ceiling on total reservations (can be exceeded in extraordinary circumstances — Tamil Nadu). Creamy layer exclusion mandatory for OBCs but not SC/ST.
VISHAKA v. STATE OF RAJASTHAN | 1997 | SC
Issued Vishaka Guidelines on prevention of sexual harassment at workplace, filling legislative vacuum. Basis for the POSH Act 2013. Held that right to work with dignity under Art. 14, 19, 21 includes freedom from sexual harassment.
JUSTICE K.S. PUTTASWAMY v. UNION OF INDIA | 2017 | 9-judge bench
Right to Privacy declared a fundamental right under Art. 21. Overruled M.P. Sharma and Kharak Singh judgments. Privacy includes bodily integrity, personal choices, informational privacy, and dignity.
NAVTEJ SINGH JOHAR v. UNION OF INDIA | 2018 | SC
Decriminalised consensual same-sex relations (Sec. 377 IPC partially struck down). Held that sexual orientation is a fundamental right under Art. 14, 15, 19, 21. Overruled Suresh Kumar Koushal (2013).
A.K. GOPALAN v. STATE OF MADRAS | 1950 | SC
First major constitutional case. Held Art. 21 to mean mere procedure established by law (even if unfair). Overruled by Maneka Gandhi (1978).
STATE OF MADRAS v. CHAMPAKAM DORAIRAJAN | 1951 | SC
First case to be decided under FR (Art. 15). State's communal reservation in educational admissions held unconstitutional. Led to 1st Constitutional Amendment adding Art. 15(4).
I.C. GOLAK NATH v. STATE OF PUNJAB | 1967 | 11-judge bench
Held Parliament cannot amend Fundamental Rights. Applied doctrine of prospective overruling for the first time. Overruled by Kesavananda Bharati (1973).
BERUBARI UNION CASE | 1960 | SC
SC held that Preamble is NOT part of the Constitution and is merely a key to open the minds of framers. Overruled in Kesavananda Bharati (1973) where SC held Preamble IS part of the Constitution.
M. NAGARAJ v. UNION OF INDIA | 2006 | SC
Upheld Art. 16(4A) & 16(4B) on SC/ST promotion reservations but added conditions: the State must demonstrate backwardness, inadequacy of representation, and that efficiency is maintained.
JANHIT ABHIYAN v. UNION OF INDIA | 2022 | 5-judge bench
Upheld 10% EWS reservation (103rd Constitutional Amendment) as constitutionally valid. Held that economic criterion alone can be basis for reservation and does not violate Basic Structure despite exceeding 50% ceiling.
SUBHASH KASHINATH MAHAJAN v. STATE OF MAHARASHTRA | 2018 | SC
Diluted SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act protections by introducing preliminary enquiry before FIR. Later reviewed and overruled by a larger bench upholding original Act's stringent provisions.

14 PYQ QUESTION CARDS

🔵 PYQ CRITICAL
Q1. Which amendment inserted the words "Socialist", "Secular" and "Integrity" in the Preamble of the Constitution?
✅ ANSWER: 42nd Constitutional Amendment Act, 1976

The 42nd Amendment (1976), passed during the Emergency period under PM Indira Gandhi, added three words: Socialist, Secular, Integrity. It is sometimes called the "Mini Constitution" as it made the most sweeping changes to the Constitution. The Preamble has been amended only once in India's constitutional history.

💎 GEM
Remember: Only 42nd Amendment changed the Preamble. The three new words make up the phrase "S-S-I" — Socialist Secular Integrity. These reflect India's socialist welfare state goals, religious neutrality, and national unity priorities of that era.
🔵 PYQ TRAP
Q2. How many Fundamental Duties are currently there in the Indian Constitution?
✅ ANSWER: 11 Fundamental Duties

TRAP ALERT: Many candidates answer 10. Originally 10 duties were added by the 42nd Amendment (1976). The 11th duty was added by the 86th Amendment (2002): "It shall be the duty of every citizen who is a parent or guardian to provide opportunities for education to his child or ward between the age of six and fourteen years." Currently 11 Fundamental Duties.

🔵 PYQ CRITICAL
Q3. Which article of the Indian Constitution is called the "Heart and Soul of the Constitution" by Dr. B.R. Ambedkar?
✅ ANSWER: Article 32 — Right to Constitutional Remedies

Dr. Ambedkar called Article 32 (Right to move the Supreme Court for enforcement of FRs) the "Heart and Soul of the Constitution." Without this article, all other FRs would be meaningless as there would be no mechanism to enforce them. Article 32 itself is a Fundamental Right and cannot be suspended during Emergency under Art. 352 (though Art. 358-359 suspends other FRs).

🔵 PYQ TRAP
Q4. A Money Bill can be introduced in which house of Parliament and what is Rajya Sabha's role?
✅ ANSWER: Only Lok Sabha; Rajya Sabha has 14 days to return it

TRAP: Rajya Sabha cannot introduce or reject Money Bills. Under Art. 109, a Money Bill can only be introduced in Lok Sabha on the recommendation of the President. After passing in Lok Sabha, it is transmitted to Rajya Sabha which has only 14 days to return it with recommendations (which Lok Sabha may or may not accept). If Rajya Sabha holds it beyond 14 days, the bill is deemed passed by both Houses.

🔵 PYQ GEM
Q5. What is the "Basic Structure Doctrine" and which case established it?
✅ ANSWER: Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)

The Basic Structure Doctrine holds that while Parliament has wide power to amend the Constitution under Art. 368, it cannot alter or destroy the "basic structure" of the Constitution. Elements of Basic Structure include: Supremacy of Constitution, Republican & Democratic form of government, Secular character, Federal structure, Separation of powers, Judicial Review, Rule of Law, Parliamentary system, Free & fair elections, and Unity & integrity of India.

💎 GEM
This was decided by a 13-judge bench by a slim 7-6 majority. It is arguably the most significant constitutional decision in Indian legal history. Justice H.R. Khanna's concurring opinion later became the majority position. The doctrine was used to strike down parts of the 42nd and 39th Amendments and continues to be the ultimate check on Parliament's amending power.
🔵 PYQ CRITICAL
Q6. How many times has National Emergency been proclaimed in India, and under what circumstances?
✅ ANSWER: Three times — 1962, 1971, 1975

1st Emergency (1962): October 26 – January 10, 1968 — during Chinese aggression (still in force during 1965 Indo-Pak war)
2nd Emergency (1971): December 3–March 21, 1977 — during Bangladesh Liberation War (Pakistani aggression)
3rd Emergency (1975): June 25–March 21, 1977 — "Internal disturbance"; most controversial; led to suspension of FRs, press censorship, political arrests. Led to 44th Amendment to prevent abuse.

🔵 PYQ TIP
Q7. Which article provides for the right to education, and what amendment inserted it?
✅ ANSWER: Article 21A, inserted by 86th Amendment (2002)

The 86th Amendment Act, 2002 inserted Art. 21A making free and compulsory education a Fundamental Right for children aged 6 to 14 years. This led to the Right to Education (RTE) Act, 2009. The same amendment also: (1) amended Art. 45 (DPSP) for early childhood care for children below 6 years; (2) added the 11th Fundamental Duty requiring parents to provide education to children aged 6–14.

🔵 PYQ TRAP
Q8. The Right to Property was removed as a Fundamental Right by which amendment? Where does it stand now?
✅ ANSWER: 44th Amendment (1978); now a Legal/Constitutional Right under Art. 300A

The 44th Amendment Act, 1978 under Janata government deleted the Right to Property from Part III (Fundamental Rights). Originally Art. 19(1)(f) and Art. 31 gave property rights. Now Art. 300A provides: "No person shall be deprived of his property save by authority of law" — a legal right, not a FR. This means property disputes go to civil courts, not directly to Supreme Court under Art. 32.

🔵 PYQ CRITICAL
Q9. What is the meaning and significance of Article 17 of the Indian Constitution?
✅ ANSWER: Abolition of Untouchability — enforceable against private individuals

Art. 17 abolishes "Untouchability" and forbids its practice in any form. Enforcement of any disability arising from untouchability is an offence punishable by law. The Protection of Civil Rights Act, 1955 and SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989 give legal teeth to Art. 17. Unique significance: most FRs are against the State, but Art. 17 is directly enforceable against private individuals too, making it a horizontal right.

🔵 PYQ GEM
Q10. What are the exclusive special powers of Rajya Sabha that Lok Sabha does not have?
✅ ANSWER: Art. 249 and Art. 312

Rajya Sabha has two exclusive powers:
Art. 249: By a resolution supported by not less than 2/3 of its members present and voting, Rajya Sabha can authorise Parliament to make laws on a State List subject if it is in the national interest. Such resolution is valid for 1 year and can be renewed.
Art. 312: Rajya Sabha can create new All India Services (like IAS, IPS) common to both Centre and States by a 2/3 majority resolution.

🔵 PYQ CRITICAL
Q11. Under Article 352, what is the required majority for Parliamentary approval of National Emergency?
✅ ANSWER: Special Majority — 2/3 of members present & voting + absolute majority of total membership

After the 44th Amendment (1978), National Emergency proclamation must be approved by both Houses within one month by special majority: not less than 2/3 of the members of each House present and voting AND a majority of the total membership of each House. The Lok Sabha can also revoke it by a simple majority passed through a special session called when at least 1/10th of Lok Sabha members requisition it.

🔵 PYQ TIP
Q12. What are the Socialistic, Gandhian, and Liberal-Intellectual DPSPs? Give one example each.
✅ ANSWER: Three categories of DPSPs

Socialistic DPSPs (welfare state): Art. 39 — equal pay for equal work, Art. 41 — right to work, Art. 42 — maternity relief, Art. 47 — raising standard of living.
Gandhian DPSPs (Gram Swaraj): Art. 40 — panchayats, Art. 43 — cottage industries, Art. 46 — upliftment of SCs/STs, Art. 48 — cow protection.
Liberal-Intellectual DPSPs: Art. 44 — Uniform Civil Code, Art. 45 — early childhood care, Art. 48A — environmental protection, Art. 51 — international peace.

🔵 PYQ TRAP
Q13. The election of the Vice-President of India is conducted by whom?
✅ ANSWER: Electoral College of both Houses of Parliament (elected + nominated members) — NOT just elected members

TRAP: Many confuse Vice-President's election with President's election.
President's election (Art. 54): Elected members of Lok Sabha + Rajya Sabha + State Assemblies + UT Assemblies (elected members only)
Vice-President's election (Art. 66): Members of BOTH Houses of Parliament — both elected AND nominated members — in a JOINT SITTING. State Assemblies do NOT participate. Single transferable vote system.

🔵 PYQ CRITICAL
Q14. The Uniform Civil Code is mentioned in which article? What is its current status?
✅ ANSWER: Article 44 — DPSP; not implemented nationwide; Goa has a partial UCC

Art. 44 directs the State to secure a Uniform Civil Code (UCC) for all citizens. Being a DPSP, it is non-justiciable. Supreme Court in Shah Bano case (1985), Sarla Mudgal case (1995), and John Vallamattom case (2003) urged Parliament to enact UCC. Uttarakhand became the first state to enact a UCC (2024). Goa is cited as the only state with a partial UCC through the Portuguese Civil Code still in operation.

🔵 PYQ GEM
Q15. What is the SR Bommai case (1994) and why is it significant for Indian federalism?
✅ ANSWER: SR Bommai v. Union of India — landmark for federalism and Art. 356

SR Bommai (CM of Karnataka) challenged dismissal of his government under Art. 356. The 9-judge bench held:
1. Federalism is part of Basic Structure — States are not mere agents of Centre
2. Art. 356 is subject to judicial review
3. Before dismissal, floor test must be conducted
4. The President acts on Cabinet's written advice
5. Proclamation must be ratified by Parliament
This case practically stopped rampant misuse of Art. 356 which was used 100+ times before 1994 for political reasons.

15 LIVE GEM BOXES

💎 LIVE GEM 1 — WHY THE BASIC STRUCTURE DOCTRINE MATTERS

Before 1973, Parliament could amend any part of the Constitution, including Fundamental Rights. The Indira Gandhi government used this to remove property rights, curtail freedoms, and concentrate power. The Basic Structure Doctrine of Kesavananda Bharati (1973) created an unwritten limit on Parliament's amending power. It has since been used to:
• Strike down the 39th Amendment (1975) which immunized PM's election from challenge
• Strike down parts of the 42nd Amendment (1976) — Minerva Mills
• Uphold federalism and judicial independence against legislative encroachment
India is unique globally — while most democracies allow complete constitutional revision, India has this judge-made doctrine ensuring constitutional continuity beyond any Parliament's political will.

💎 LIVE GEM 2 — HOW SR BOMMAI TRANSFORMED INDIAN FEDERALISM

Between 1950–1994, Article 356 (President's Rule) was imposed over 100 times, often for blatant political reasons — to dismiss opposition-run governments. The Sarkaria Commission (1988) recommended reforms. When SR Bommai's government was dismissed in 1989, the case took 5 years to reach its landmark verdict. Post-1994, impositions of Article 356 dropped dramatically. The doctrine of floor test before dismissal, judicial review of proclamations, and Parliament's mandatory ratification created robust checks. India's federal democracy became substantially more stable — not through constitutional amendment but through judicial interpretation.

💎 LIVE GEM 3 — ART. 21 — THE EXPANDING UNIVERSE OF RIGHTS

Article 21 simply reads: "No person shall be deprived of his life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law." Yet through judicial interpretation — especially post-Maneka Gandhi (1978) — it has become the most expansive article in the Constitution. The Supreme Court has read into Art. 21:
• Right to privacy (Puttaswamy, 2017)
• Right to live with dignity
• Right to a clean environment
• Right to speedy trial
• Right to free legal aid
• Right to livelihood
• Right to education (later given express FR status by Art. 21A)
• Right to health and medical care
• Right to die with dignity
This expansive reading is unique — the US has struggled with implied rights, but India's SC has been willing to find them through purposive interpretation, making Art. 21 truly the "heart of the Constitution."

💎 LIVE GEM 4 — THE CURIOUS CASE OF INDIA'S FEDERALISM

India is not a classical federation like the USA (where states came together) nor a unitary state like the UK. It has been called a "quasi-federal" state, a "federation with a unitary bias," or "cooperative federalism." Key features that show India's unique federal character:
• Single citizenship (unlike USA's dual citizenship)
• Residuary powers with Centre (unlike USA where they're with states)
• Single Constitution for Centre and States
• Union List has defence, foreign affairs, atomic energy
• But: States have their own legislatures, High Courts, and budget
• Seventh Schedule gives States real areas of governance
• GST (101st Amend) created shared financial governance
The term "Union of States" (not "Federation of States") in Art. 1 was deliberate — Ambedkar noted that states had no role in creating the Union, unlike American states.

💎 LIVE GEM 5 — THE DPSP-FRs TENSION AND ITS RESOLUTION

The Constitutional drafters envisioned FRs and DPSPs as complementary — individual liberty and state welfare. But clashes were inevitable:
• State of Madras v. Champakam (1951) — FRs prevail → 1st Amendment added Art. 15(4) for backward class reservations
• Golaknath (1967) — FRs cannot be abridged even for DPSPs
• Kesavananda (1973) + 24th Amendment — Parliament can amend FRs
• 42nd Amendment (1976) — Art. 31C gave total primacy to DPSPs
• Minerva Mills (1980) — SC held that the balance between FRs and DPSPs is itself part of Basic Structure. Neither can be sacrificed for the other.
Today's understanding: the Constitution envisions a welfare state that also respects individual liberty — the tension itself keeps the constitutional dialogue alive.

💎 LIVE GEM 6 — PANCHAYATI RAJ: THE THIRD TIER OF DEMOCRACY

Gandhi's vision of village swaraj finally found constitutional status through the 73rd Amendment (1992). Key constitutional innovations:
• Mandatory 3-tier structure: Gram Panchayat, Panchayat Samiti, Zila Parishad
• Mandatory 1/3 reservation for women (some states 50%)
• State Finance Commission + State Election Commission mandatory
• 11th Schedule: 29 functions devolved (agriculture, primary education, drinking water, sanitation, etc.)
• Direct elections at all 3 tiers
India now has the world's largest democratic institution at the grassroots — over 30 lakh elected representatives, with 10+ lakh women, making it the biggest experiment in participatory democracy globally. Yet "devolution without resources" remains a challenge — most Panchayats lack financial autonomy.
