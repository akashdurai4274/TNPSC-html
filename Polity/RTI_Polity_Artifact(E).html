SECTION 03

Core Sub-Topic Structural Breakdown

MODULE 01 Historical Genesis & Colonial Departures

During the colonial era, British administrative systems relied on the Official Secrets Act 1923 to maintain absolute systemic opacity. This setup institutionalized the Red Tape System—where strategic files were bound with red ribbon, restricting administrative access strictly to trusted British personnel, thus keeping Indian bureaucrats and citizens entirely out of the loop. To replace this legacy, the Union Government initially enacted the weak Freedom of Information Act 2002, which was subsequently repealed and upgraded into the robust, punitive Right to Information Act 2005.

[PYQ ALERT] 🏆 Tamil Nadu’s Legislative Precedence

TNPSC papers frequently target this timeline: Tamil Nadu was the absolute pioneer state in India, passing its own state-level RTI Act on May 4, 1997 long before the Union Government enacted the 2005 law.

[MEMORY TRICK] The 12-10 Synchronicity Link

Tie these two distinct TNPSC syllabus landmarks together:
• October 12, 2004 (12-10-2004): Tamil is officially designated as a Classical Language (Semmozhi).
• October 12, 2005 (12-10-2005): The Central RTI Act 2005 officially enters active enforcement.
Mnemonic: Exactly 1 year to the day after Tamil became a classical language, the RTI Act empowered citizens nationwide.

MODULE 02 Institutional Jurisdictions & Blanket Exemptions

The RTI Act covers all Public Authorities, which include constitutional bodies, statutory setups formed by Parliament or State Legislatures, local bodies, and non-governmental organizations (NGOs) that receive substantial direct or indirect government funding.

[CRITICAL FACT] Judicial Transparency Shift

Per the landmark November 13, 2019 Supreme Court ruling confirming a previous Delhi High Court judgment, the office of the Chief Justice of India (CJI) is explicitly ruled to fall within the ambit of the RTI Act 2005 to ensure judicial accountability.

Conversely, the Act grants full immunity to strategic national security, intelligence, and fiscal enforcement units under Section 24 read along with the Second Schedule.

[TNPSC TRAP] Private Individuals & Security Ambits

Wrong Choice: Selecting Option A in questions asking if you can file an RTI against a private neighbor or check a deployment plan of the BSF.
Correct Rule: RTI is strictly applicable to Government financial channels or public offices. Private citizens cannot be targeted, and national security organizations are completely exempt unless there are specific allegations of corruption or human rights violations.

MODULE 03 Procedural Mechanics, Timeline Limits & Financial Penalties

The process requires an application fee of ₹10, payable via cash receipt, Treasury Challan (கருவூல ரசீது), or Court Fee Stamp (நீதிமன்ற வில்லை). A critical statutory mandate protects the citizen's privacy: the applicant is required to provide only their name and communication address, and cannot be forced to state the reason for seeking the information.

[CRITICAL FACT] Special Protection for Illiterate Applicants

If a citizen is unable to read or write, they cannot be turned away. The law dictates that the Public Information Officer (PIO) or Assistant PIO must provide complete assistance to transcribe their oral request into a valid written application.

Failure to provide information within the mandatory statutory limits triggers the automatic activation of heavy penalty clauses.

Procedural Default Condition Statutory Penalty / Redressal Ambit
Delays by the PIO without valid justification ₹250 per day up to a ceiling of ₹25,000
Standard Response Window Missed First Appeal to Senior Officer within 30 days
[EXPANDED] Second Appeal Window Final appeal to Central/State Information Commission within 90 days
[PYQ ALERT] 🏆 The Historic First Application Landmark

The very first formal RTI application under the 2005 Act was filed at a police station in Pune, Maharashtra by Shahid Raza Burney on October 12, 2005. He requested detailed documentation concerning voter list discrepancies from the 2004 Lok Sabha elections.

MODULE 04 Administrative Architecture & The 2019 Amendment Shifts

The structural composition of the Central Information Commission (CIC) and State Information Commissions (SIC) consists of 1 Chief Information Commissioner and a maximum of 10 Information Commissioners. Initially, the commissions launched with a leaner composition of 1 Chief and 4 Commissioners [EXPANDED].

Commission Level Appointing Authority Selection Advisory Board Composition
Central (CIC) President of India 1. Prime Minister (Chairperson)
2. Leader of the Opposition in Lok Sabha
3. A Union Cabinet Minister nominated by the PM.
State (SIC) Governor of the State 1. Chief Minister (Chairperson)
2. Leader of the Opposition in Legislative Assembly
3. A State Cabinet Minister nominated by the CM.
[CRITICAL FACT] Reappointment Restrictions & Loophole Exemptions

To preserve institutional integrity, the Chief and ordinary Information Commissioners are strictly ineligible for reappointment to the same office after completing their term. Crucial Exception: An ordinary Information Commissioner can be elevated to Chief Information Commissioner, provided their total combined tenure does not exceed 5 years.

[CONNECT] Parity with the Election Commission Matrix

Originally, the rank, remuneration, and structural benefits of the Central and State Information Commissioners matched those of the Chief Election Commissioner and State Election Commissioners, respectively. They wield full civil court powers—allowing them to issue summonses, demand affidavits, and compel the production of public documents.

[TNPSC TRAP] The 2019 Structural Tenure Redefinition

Old Rule Trap: Choosing a fixed statutory term of 5 years or 65 years as an unalterable rule for all commissioners.
2019 Amendment Reality: The RTI (Amendment) Act 2019 stripped away the absolute parity with Election Commissioners. The term length (not exceeding 5 years) and salaries are now determined by the Central Government, changing the long-standing status quo. The 65-year upper age limit remains unchanged.

SECTION 07

High-Yield MCQ Diagnostic Drill

Q1. Which state was the first in India to introduce a dedicated Right to Information Act?
A) Maharashtra
B) Goa
C) Tamil Nadu
D) Karnataka

✅ Answer: C — Tamil Nadu

📌 Why: Tamil Nadu passed its pioneering state-level RTI Act on May 4, 1997.

🪤 Trap: Students often mistake the site of the first application (Pune, Maharashtra) for the first state to pass the law.

Q2. An RTI application regarding an individual's life or personal liberty must be answered within:
A) 24 Hours
B) 48 Hours
C) 7 Days
D) 30 Days

✅ Answer: B — 48 Hours

📌 Why: Section 7(1) mandates a strict 48-hour deadline if a citizen's life or liberty is at stake.

🪤 Trap: Falling back on the standard 30-day response window by failing to note the emergency condition.

Q3. Who chairs the high-level selection panel that recommends the appointment of State Information Commissioners?
A) The Governor
B) The Chief Minister
C) The Chief Justice of the High Court
D) The Speaker of the Assembly

✅ Answer: B — The Chief Minister

📌 Why: The Chief Minister leads the selection committee, while the Governor serves as the formal appointing authority.

🪤 Trap: Confusing the committee chairperson (Chief Minister) with the official appointing authority (Governor).

Q4. What is the maximum total penalty that can be imposed on a PIO for delaying an application without reasonable cause?
A) ₹5,000
B) ₹10,000
C) ₹25,000
D) ₹50,000

✅ Answer: C — ₹25,000

📌 Why: The penalty accumulates at a rate of ₹250 per day, capping out at a maximum statutory limit of ₹25,000.

Q5. Which country was the absolute global pioneer in institutionalizing the Right to Information?
A) United States
B) United Kingdom
C) Sweden
D) France

✅ Answer: C — Sweden

📌 Why: Sweden established the historical precedent for open access to public documents in 1766.

Q6. Under the RTI Act, what information is an applicant legally required to provide?
A) The reason for requesting the information
B) Proof of employment status
C) Name and contact address details only
D) Income Tax returns

✅ Answer: C — Name and contact address details only

📌 Why: The law protects applicant privacy, requiring only essential contact details to deliver the requested information.

Q7. Who was the first Chief Information Commissioner of India?
A) Deepak Sandhu
B) Wajahat Habibullah
C) Rajkumar Goyal
D) M.S. Akhtar

✅ Answer: B — Wajahat Habibullah

📌 Why: Wajahat Habibullah served as India's inaugural CIC following the Act's launch in 2005.

Q8. Which section of the RTI Act allows the ordinary Information Commissioner to be elevated to Chief Commissioner?
A) Section 12 Ambit
B) Section 24 Exception Ambit
C) Reappointment Elevation Clause
D) Section 5 Directive

✅ Answer: C — Reappointment Elevation Clause

📌 Why: Commissioners cannot be reappointed to the same role, but ordinary commissioners can be elevated to Chief, subject to the 5-year maximum tenure limit.

Q9. Which agency is completely exempt from standard RTI disclosures under the Second Schedule?
A) NITI Aayog
B) Research and Analysis Wing (RAW)
C) Life Insurance Corporation (LIC)
D) BSNL Corporation

✅ Answer: B — Research and Analysis Wing (RAW)

📌 Why: RAW is classified as a premier strategic intelligence organization, granting it full exemption under Section 24.

Q10. What is the maximum age limit for an Information Commissioner holding office?
A) 62 Years
B) 65 Years
C) 68 Years
D) 70 Years

✅ Answer: B — 65 Years

📌 Why: While the 2019 amendment modified term lengths, the upper age limit remains fixed at 65 years.
