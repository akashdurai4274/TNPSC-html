Section 01
Topic Overview

Adjectives are words that describe a noun (a naming word) by giving additional meaning or information about it. In Parts of Speech (the backbone of English Grammar for TNPSC), Adjectives are one of 8 parts. Mastering adjective types helps you answer fill-in-the-blank, identify-the-part-of-speech, and error-correction questions that appear across all TNPSC groups.

Core Rule to Remember
⚡ CRITICAL FACT

Core Definition: An adjective is a word that describes a noun. It gives additional (extra) information about the noun. The adjective always appears before the noun (attributive position) OR after a linking verb like "is/are/was" (predicative position).

Example: "Good boy" — "Good" describes the noun "boy" → Adjective
Example: "Beautiful girl" — "Beautiful" describes the noun "girl" → Adjective

🧠 MEMORY TRICK

Parts of Speech covered so far: N–P–V–A = Noun → Pronoun → Verb → Adjective. Think: "No Problem, Very Able!"

Numbers alone ≠ Adjective. The number "6" by itself is NOT an adjective. Only when a number appears BEFORE a noun does it become an Adjective of Number.
❌ Wrong: "6" (alone) = adjective  |  ✅ Correct: "6 tables" → "6" = adjective of number.

Section 03
Type 1 — Adjective of Quality
RULE 01
Definition
An Adjective of Quality describes the quality or character of a noun. It answers the question: "What kind of?"
Tamil: குவாலிட்டி = தரம் (quality/character)
RULE 02
Position
Usually placed immediately before the noun. Also valid after linking verbs (is, are, was, were).
e.g. "She is beautiful" → predicative adjective
NounAdjective of QualityMeaningFull Sentence
BoyGoodkind, well-behavedGood boy
GirlBeautifulattractive, prettyBeautiful girl
BoyBrilliantvery intelligentBrilliant boy
BookInterestingengaging, captivatingInteresting book
BoyCuteadorable, sweetCute boy
CityBiglarge, vastChennai is a big city

Practice sentences from lecture:

  • Ramu is a brilliant boy. → brilliant = Adjective of Quality
  • She is a beautiful girl. → beautiful = Adjective of Quality
  • Chennai is a big city. → big = Adjective of Quality
Q. Identify the Adjective of Quality: "She is a beautiful girl."
A) She
B) is
C) beautiful ✅
D) girl
✅ Answer: C
📌 "beautiful" describes the quality of the noun "girl".
🪤 Trap: Students often mark "girl" — but "girl" is the NOUN, not the adjective.
🏆 PYQ ALERT

TNPSC Group 4 (2019, 2022) asked: "Identify the adjective in: 'He is a tall man.'" The answer is tall (quality). Distractors included "man" and "He". The structure [Adjective + Noun] is a recurring pattern.

Section 04
Type 2 — Adjective of Quantity
RULE 03
Definition
An Adjective of Quantity describes how much of a noun is present. It answers the question: "How much?"
Tamil: குவான்டிட்டி = அளவு (amount/measure)
RULE 04
Used With
Used with uncountable nouns (nouns you cannot count individually): milk, sand, furniture, food, water, air, knowledge.
Tamil: அன்கவுண்டபிள் நவுன் = எண்ண முடியாத பெயர்ச்சொல்
Adjective of QuantityMeaningNoun (Uncountable)Example
enoughsufficient amountmilk, food, sandenough milk
somea little / certain amountfurniture, watersome furniture
nozero amountidea, foodno idea
littlesmall amountmoney, timelittle time
muchlarge amountnoise, informationmuch noise
sufficientadequate amountspace, evidencesufficient evidence

Lecture Examples:

  • I have enough milk to prepare tea.
  • There is some furniture in my house.
  • I have no idea. (No = Adjective of Quantity)
  • I have enough sand. (Sand = uncountable noun)
  • I have no food to eat.
🪤 TNPSC TRAP

Quantity vs Number: "No idea" uses Quantity because "idea" is uncountable. "No dogs" could work as Number. The key is whether the noun is countable or uncountable.
❌ Wrong: "Some chairs" = Quantity  |  ✅ Correct: "Some chairs" = Number (chairs are countable → use Adjective of Number)

🧠 MEMORY TRICK

QUANTUM words = Quantity Adjectives (for uncountable nouns):
E–S–N–L–M = Enough · Some · No · Little · Much
Remember: "Every Swimmer Needs Large Muscles"

Q. Which adjective type is used in: "I have no food to eat"?
A) Adjective of Number
B) Adjective of Quantity ✅
C) Demonstrative Adjective
D) Possessive Adjective
✅ Answer: B
📌 "food" is an uncountable noun; "no" tells us the amount (zero) → Adjective of Quantity.
🪤 Trap: Students pick "Number" thinking "no" relates to counting.
Section 05
Type 3 — Adjective of Number
RULE 05
Definition
An Adjective of Number tells us how many of a noun are present. It answers: "How many?" Used with countable nouns.
Tamil: நம்பர் = எண்ணிக்கை (count)
RULE 06
Critical Condition
A number becomes an adjective ONLY when a noun follows it. A standalone number (e.g., just "6") is NOT an adjective.
"6" alone = NOT adjective | "6 tables" = 6 IS adjective
Adjective of NumberTypeNoun (Countable)Example
fiveDefinite numeralpensfive pens
threeDefinite numeralbooksthree books
twoDefinite numeraldogstwo dogs
sixDefinite numeralpencilssix pencils
manyIndefinite numeralshirts, sareesmany shirts
fewIndefinite numeralstudents, chairsfew students

Lecture Practice Sentences:

  • I have two dogs. → "two" = Adjective of Number
  • She has six pencils. → "six" = Adjective of Number
  • My mother has many sarees. → "many" = Adjective of Number
  • I have three chocolates. → "three" = Adjective of Number
⚡ CRITICAL FACT

Two sub-types of Adjective of Number:
1. Definite Numeral Adjective — exact number: one, two, three, first, second (ordinals also qualify)
2. Indefinite Numeral Adjective — approximate number: many, few, some, several, all, no

[EXPANDED — not explicitly stated in lecture but required for TNPSC Group 1/2]

🧠 MEMORY TRICK

Number adjectives = always followed by a COUNTABLE noun
Test: Can you put "one, two, three…" before the noun? If YES → countable → Adjective of Number applies.
"One milk?" ❌ → uncountable → use Quantity | "One dog?" ✅ → countable → use Number

Q. Identify the adjective type in: "My mother has many sarees."
A) Adjective of Quality
B) Adjective of Quantity
C) Adjective of Number ✅
D) Possessive Adjective
✅ Answer: C
📌 "sarees" is a countable noun; "many" tells the number/count → Adjective of Number.
🪤 Trap: "many" looks like a quantity word, so students pick Quantity. But sarees are countable!
Section 06
Type 4 — Demonstrative Adjective
RULE 07
Definition
A Demonstrative Adjective points to or specifies a particular noun. It demonstrates (indicates) which specific person or thing is being referred to.
Tamil: குறிப்பிட்டு சொல்வது = pointing/specifying
RULE 08
The Four Words
This · That · These · Those
Near singular: this | Far singular: that
Near plural: these | Far plural: those
Must be FOLLOWED by a noun to be an adjective
WordDistanceNumberExampleNote
thisNearSingularthis pen, this bookAdjective only if noun follows
thatFarSingularthat boy, that bagAdjective only if noun follows
theseNearPluralthese books, these boysAdjective only if noun follows
thoseFarPluralthose pens, those girlsAdjective only if noun follows

Lecture Practice Sentences:

  • This book is mine. → "this" = Demonstrative Adjective
  • These boys are my friends. → "these" = Demonstrative Adjective
  • Those girls are my classmates. → "those" = Demonstrative Adjective
  • That boy is my friend. → "that" = Demonstrative Adjective
🪤 TNPSC TRAP

"This is mine" ≠ Demonstrative Adjective!
"This is mine" → this is used as a PRONOUN (it replaces a noun, nothing follows it).
"This book is mine" → this is used as a DEMONSTRATIVE ADJECTIVE (noun "book" follows it).

❌ Wrong: "This is my bag" → "this" = demonstrative adjective
✅ Correct: "This bag is mine" → "this" = demonstrative adjective | "This is mine" → "this" = pronoun

🔗 CONNECT

Demonstrative Pronoun vs Demonstrative Adjective: This distinction is tested in TNPSC! When "this/that/these/those" stands alone (no noun after it), it is a Demonstrative Pronoun. When a noun follows, it is a Demonstrative Adjective. Cross-topic link to Pronouns chapter.

Q. In which sentence is "this" a Demonstrative ADJECTIVE (not a pronoun)?
A) "This is my pen."
B) "This pen is mine." ✅
C) "This is beautiful."
D) "What is this?"
✅ Answer: B
📌 Only in B does "this" directly precede the noun "pen" — making it an adjective.
🪤 Trap: A is the most commonly chosen wrong answer — "this" appears before "is" not a noun.
Section 07
Type 5 — Interrogative Adjective
RULE 09
Definition
An Interrogative Adjective is used to ask a question about a noun. It introduces a question relating to the noun that follows it.
Tamil: இன்டரோகேட்டிவ் = கேள்வி (question)
RULE 10
The Three Words
What · Which · Whose
— "What color do you like?" (open choice)
— "Which movie is your favourite?" (limited options)
— "Whose pen is this?" (ownership)
WordUsed WhenExampleAdjective Here
whatOpen-ended question (any option)What color do you like?what
whichLimited/specific options givenWhich movie is your favourite?which
whoseAsking about ownershipWhose book is this?whose
⚡ CRITICAL FACT

What vs Which:
What color do you like? → Asking from ALL possible colors in the world (unlimited choice)
Which color do you like? → Red or Blue? (asking from given limited options)

Both "what" and "which" can be Interrogative Adjectives when followed by a noun.

🪤 TNPSC TRAP

Standalone "what/which/whose" = NOT Adjective
"What?" alone = NOT an adjective (it's a pronoun/adverb)
"Which?" alone = NOT an adjective
They become Interrogative Adjectives ONLY when a noun follows them.

❌ "What is that?" → "what" = pronoun
✅ "What movie is that?" → "what" = Interrogative Adjective (noun "movie" follows)

Q. Identify the Interrogative Adjective: "Whose book is this?"
A) Whose ✅
B) book
C) is
D) this
✅ Answer: A
📌 "whose" asks about ownership of the noun "book" → Interrogative Adjective.
🪤 Trap: "this" also looks like an adjective but here it follows "is" (predicate), not a noun directly.
Section 08
Type 6 — Possessive Adjective
RULE 11
Definition
A Possessive Adjective shows ownership or possession. It tells us to whom the noun belongs. Always placed before a noun.
Tamil: பொசஸிவ் = உரிமை கொண்டாடுவது (claiming ownership)
RULE 12
All Possessive Adjectives
my · your · his · her · its · our · their
Must be followed by a noun to function as adjective
Possessive AdjectivePersonExampleMeaning
my1st person singularmy book, my car, my sisterbelonging to me
your2nd personyour bagbelonging to you
his3rd person singular (male)his bike, his friendbelonging to him
her3rd person singular (female)her bag, her carbelonging to her
its3rd person singular (thing/animal)its tailbelonging to it
our1st person pluralour motherlandbelonging to us
their3rd person pluraltheir housebelonging to them

Lecture Examples:

  • This is my car. → "my" = Possessive Adjective
  • It is her bag. → "her" = Possessive Adjective
  • India is our motherland. → "our" = Possessive Adjective
  • It is his bike. → "his" = Possessive Adjective
  • He is my brother. → "my" = Possessive Adjective
🪤 TNPSC TRAP

Possessive Adjective vs Object Pronoun:
"I call him" → "him" = Object Pronoun (NOT adjective — no noun follows)
"I call her friend" → "her" = Possessive Adjective (noun "friend" follows)

Key rule: his/her/my/our = Adjective ONLY when followed by a noun. Alone, they are pronouns.

🔗 CONNECT

Possessive Adjective vs Possessive Pronoun:
"This is my car" → my = Possessive ADJECTIVE (noun follows)
"This car is mine" → mine = Possessive PRONOUN (no noun follows; it replaces "my car")
This distinction appears in TNPSC pronoun-vs-adjective questions!

Q. Which sentence uses "her" as a Possessive Adjective?
A) "I called her."
B) "It is her bag." ✅
C) "She helped her."
D) "He saw her."
✅ Answer: B
📌 Only in B does "her" precede the noun "bag" — showing possession.
🪤 Trap: In A, C, D — "her" is an object pronoun, not followed by a noun.
Section 09
Type 7 — Proper Adjective
RULE 13
Definition
A Proper Adjective is derived from a Proper Noun (name of a specific place, country, person). It specifies the origin or particular type of the noun.
Tamil: குறிப்பிட்ட இடம்/நாட்டிலிருந்து வருவது
RULE 14
Always Capitalized
Proper Adjectives are always written with a capital letter because they are derived from proper nouns (names of countries/places).
India → Indian | China → Chinese | America → American
Proper Noun (Country/Place)Proper AdjectiveExample
IndiaIndianIndian food, Indian boy
ChinaChineseChinese food, Chinese movie, Chinese girl
AmericaAmericanAmerican boy, American culture
Tamil NaduTamilTamil movie, Tamil food
EnglandEnglishEnglish grammar, English film
JapanJapaneseJapanese car, Japanese technology

Lecture Examples:

  • I like Indian food. → "Indian" = Proper Adjective
  • I like Chinese food. → "Chinese" = Proper Adjective
  • He is an American boy. → "American" = Proper Adjective
  • She is a Chinese girl. → "Chinese" = Proper Adjective
  • My neighbour is an American boy. → "American" = Proper Adjective
🪤 TNPSC TRAP

"I am an Indian" ≠ Proper Adjective!
"I am an Indian" → "Indian" = Proper Noun (used as a noun here, refers to a person's identity)
"I am an Indian man" → "Indian" = Proper Adjective (noun "man" follows it)

Same logic as all other adjective types: a noun must follow!

🔗 CONNECT

Proper Noun → Proper Adjective: This connects to the Nouns chapter. Proper Nouns (India, China) when placed before another noun to describe it become Proper Adjectives. This overlap is a favorite TNPSC testing point across Group 2 and Group 4 exams.

Q. In "I like Chinese food," what type of adjective is "Chinese"?
A) Adjective of Quality
B) Demonstrative Adjective
C) Proper Adjective ✅
D) Possessive Adjective
✅ Answer: C
📌 "Chinese" is derived from the proper noun "China" and describes the noun "food".
🪤 Trap: "Quality" seems right because it describes what kind of food, but the origin test reveals it's Proper Adjective.
Section 10
Type 8 — Distributive Adjective
RULE 15
Definition
A Distributive Adjective refers to each individual or member of a group separately. It distributes the meaning across members of a group.
Tamil: குழுவில் ஒவ்வொன்னையும் தனித்தனியாக சொல்வது
RULE 16
Key Words
each · every · either · neither · any · both · none
When ANY of these words precede a noun, they become Distributive Adjectives.
WordMeaningExample
eachevery one individuallyEach boy must bring his own pen.
everyall members without exceptionI clean every day. / Every student was present.
eitherone of two (choice)Either side is fine.
neithernot one, not the other (of two)Neither book is useful.
boththe two togetherBoth of them are in the house.
anyone from a group (unspecified)Any student can answer.
nonenot one / not anyNone of the pens work.

Lecture Examples:

  • I clean every day. → "every" = Distributive Adjective (noun "day" follows)
  • Both of them are in the house. → "both" = Distributive Adjective
  • Each boy must bring his own pen. → "each" = Distributive Adjective
  • Every student was present. → "every" = Distributive Adjective
🧠 MEMORY TRICK

Distributive Adjective key words: E–E–E–N–B–A–N
Each · Every · Either · Neither · Both · Any · None
Memory sentence: "Even Eels Either Nibble, Bite, And Nip"

🪤 TNPSC TRAP

"Every" vs "Each": Both are Distributive Adjectives, but:
Each = used when counting/referring to individual members (smaller groups)
Every = used for larger groups; implies "all without exception"
In TNPSC fill-in-the-blank, "every" takes singular verb: "Every student was present" ✅

Q. Identify the Distributive Adjective: "Every student was present."
A) Every ✅
B) student
C) was
D) present
✅ Answer: A
📌 "Every" precedes noun "student" and distributes meaning across each member → Distributive Adjective.
🪤 Trap: "present" looks like an adjective (predicative) — and it is! But the question asks for Distributive specifically.

Section 13
Quick MCQ Drill — 10 Questions
Q1. Which word is an Adjective of Quality in: "Chennai is a big city"?
A) Chennai
B) is
C) big ✅
D) city
✅ Answer: C — big
📌 "big" describes the quality (size) of the noun "city".
🪤 Trap: "city" is the noun, not the adjective. "Chennai" is a proper noun.
Q2. "I have no food to eat." — The adjective "no" belongs to which type?
A) Distributive Adjective
B) Adjective of Quantity ✅
C) Adjective of Number
D) Demonstrative Adjective
✅ Answer: B
📌 "food" is uncountable; "no" indicates zero amount → Quantity.
🪤 Trap: "no" also appears in Distributive ("none") which confuses students.
Q3. What type of adjective is "six" in: "She has six pencils"?
A) Adjective of Quantity
B) Adjective of Number ✅
C) Adjective of Quality
D) Distributive Adjective
✅ Answer: B
📌 "pencils" are countable; "six" tells the exact count → Number adjective.
🪤 Trap: Numbers feel like "quantities" but are classified as Number adjectives for countable nouns.
Q4. In "These books are mine," what is the adjective type of "these"?
A) Possessive Adjective
B) Interrogative Adjective
C) Demonstrative Adjective ✅
D) Distributive Adjective
✅ Answer: C
📌 "these" points to specific books (near, plural) before the noun "books".
🪤 Trap: "mine" looks like a possessive — but "mine" is a possessive PRONOUN, not adjective here.
Q5. "Whose bike is this?" — What type is "whose"?
A) Possessive Adjective
B) Interrogative Adjective ✅
C) Demonstrative Adjective
D) Proper Adjective
✅ Answer: B
📌 "whose" asks a question about the noun "bike" → Interrogative Adjective.
🪤 Trap: "whose" shows possession, so students mark it Possessive — but possession in a QUESTION = Interrogative.
Q6. "India is our motherland." — Identify the adjective type of "our".
A) Possessive Adjective ✅
B) Demonstrative Adjective
C) Proper Adjective
D) Distributive Adjective
✅ Answer: A
📌 "our" shows collective ownership of "motherland" → Possessive Adjective.
🪤 Trap: "India" might distract students into choosing Proper Adjective, but "India" here is the subject noun, not an adjective.
Q7. "I like Indian food." — What type of adjective is "Indian"?
A) Adjective of Quality
B) Demonstrative Adjective
C) Proper Adjective ✅
D) Possessive Adjective
✅ Answer: C
📌 "Indian" is derived from proper noun "India" and specifies the type of "food".
🪤 Trap: "Indian" describes the food, so "Quality" seems right — but it's derived from a proper noun → Proper Adjective.
Q8. Which sentence uses a Distributive Adjective correctly?
A) "This book is mine."
B) "She is a beautiful girl."
C) "Each boy must bring his pen." ✅
D) "I have five pens."
✅ Answer: C
📌 "each" distributes the action to every individual "boy" → Distributive Adjective.
🪤 Trap: "his" in option C is also an adjective (possessive)! The question specifically asks about distributive.
Q9. In "I call her" — why is "her" NOT an adjective?
A) Because "her" cannot be an adjective at all
B) Because it is in a question sentence
C) Because no noun follows "her" ✅
D) Because "I" is the subject
✅ Answer: C
📌 For possessive adjectives, a noun MUST follow. "I call her" — "her" is followed by nothing → it is an object pronoun.
🪤 Trap: Students know "her" = possessive adjective from other examples and apply it everywhere.
Q10. What is the ONE rule that applies to ALL 8 types of adjectives?
A) They always end in "-ful" or "-ous"
B) They always come after the verb
C) They always relate to / describe a noun ✅
D) They only describe animate nouns (people, animals)
✅ Answer: C
📌 All adjectives — regardless of type — describe, qualify, or give additional information about a NOUN.
🪤 Trap: This conceptual question is asked in Group 2 Paper II. Students who memorise types without the core rule miss it.

🎯 EXAMINER'S TRAP MCQ — 2026 PREDICTION
Q. In the sentence "She is her teacher," what type of adjective is "her"?
A) Demonstrative Adjective
B) Possessive Adjective ✅
C) Object Pronoun — NOT an adjective
D) Interrogative Adjective
Why this is the trap: Most students will pick C — "Object Pronoun" because they remember the rule "her alone = pronoun, not adjective." But here "her" directly precedes the noun "teacher"! The noun "teacher" follows "her" → making it a Possessive Adjective.

Correct logic: "her teacher" = her + noun → Possessive Adjective ✅
"I see her" = her alone (no noun follows) → Object Pronoun ✅

QUALITY

  • What kind? = Quality
  • good, big, beautiful
  • brilliant, cute, tall
  • Any noun type

QUANTITY

  • How much? = Quantity
  • enough, some, no, little
  • UNCOUNTABLE nouns only
  • milk, sand, furniture

NUMBER

  • How many? = Number
  • 1, 2, 3, many, few
  • COUNTABLE nouns only
  • pens, books, chairs

DEMONSTRATIVE

  • this / that / these / those
  • Points to specific noun
  • Near: this/these
  • Far: that/those

INTERROGATIVE

  • what / which / whose
  • Asks Q about noun
  • what = open choice
  • which = limited options

POSSESSIVE

  • my/your/his/her/its
  • our / their
  • Shows ownership
  • Noun MUST follow

PROPER

  • Indian, Chinese, American
  • From proper noun
  • Always capitalized
  • India → Indian food

DISTRIBUTIVE

  • each / every / either
  • neither / both / any / none
  • Refers to each member
  • Noun must follow

BIG TRAP

  • Number alone ≠ adjective
  • "6" alone = not adj
  • "6 tables" = 6 IS adj
  • Same for this/her/whose

HOMEWORK ANSWERS

  • "good" student → Quality
  • "five" pens → Number
  • "my" friend → Possessive
