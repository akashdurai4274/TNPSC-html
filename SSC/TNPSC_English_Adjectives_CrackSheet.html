Section 01
Topic Overview — Why Adjectives Matter for TNPSC
What is an Adjective? Any word that qualifies, describes, or gives extra information about a noun or pronoun is called an adjective. Adjectives tell us about the quality, quantity, number, or identity of a noun/pronoun.

Formula: Adjective + Noun → beautiful girl, heavy object, smart person, rich man
⚡ Critical Fact
Adjectives qualify nouns and pronouns only. Adverbs qualify verbs, adjectives, and other adverbs. This distinction is tested repeatedly in Error Spotting questions.
🏆 PYQ Alert
TNPSC regularly tests adjective vs adverb confusion, degree of comparison errors, and correct use of little/a little/the little, few/a few/the few in Sentence Correction sections.

Adjectives of Quality
Definition
What Adjectives of Quality Describe
Adjectives of quality describe the characteristics of a person, place, or thing. They answer the question "What kind?" or "What type?"
CategoryWhat It DescribesExamples
SizeDimensions of the nountall, short, big, small, long, high, fat, thin
ConditionState / state of repairnew, old, clean, dirty, good, bad, broken
TasteFlavour descriptionsweet, bitter, sour, salty, spicy
ColourVisible colourred, green, black, white, blue, golden
ShapePhysical formrectangular, round, circular, square, triangular
Material / OriginWhat it is made of / where it comes fromplastic, cotton, silk, paper, wooden; Mexican, French, British, Italian, American

🪤 TNPSC Trap
Wrong: "Radha felt happily to learn that I got a job."
Why wrong: happily ends in -ly, which students assume makes it an adverb automatically. But here, the verb is felt (a linking verb). Linking verbs take adjective as complement, not adverb.
Correct: "Radha felt happy to learn that I got a job."
🧠 Memory Trick
LINKING VERBS take ADJECTIVES: feel, seem, look, appear, become, taste, smell, sound, remain, stay, grow.
Trick: "FLABS TSSRG" → Feel, Look, Appear, Become, Smell, Taste, Sound, Seem, Remain, Grow
Section 04
Adjectives of Quantity
Core Rule
Adjectives of Quantity → Always with UNCOUNTABLE Nouns
Adjectives of quantity tell how much of something there is, but do NOT give an exact number. They are used exclusively with uncountable nouns (water, sugar, wheat, milk, time, wealth, petrol).

Key words: much · little · a little · the little · some · any · enough · all · sufficient · no
⚡ Critical Fact
He has lost all her wealth. → wealth = uncountable → Adjective of Quantity
All soldiers must get here by 4 PM. → soldiers = countable → Adjective of Number
Quantity WordMeaningCorrect ExampleTNPSC Trap
littleNegative — almost nothing"There is little hope of his recovery." (= no hope almost)Students use "a little" thinking it's polite — changes meaning!
a littlePositive — some, though not much"There is a little hope of his recovery." (= some hope)Students use "little" — negates the meaning
the littleAll of the small amount available"He spent the little money he had."Confused with "a little"
muchLarge uncountable amount"How much petrol do you need?"Using "much" with countable nouns
enoughSufficient amount"I have enough wheat." / "There are enough bullets."Using "enough" after noun: "bullets enough" ✗
🏆 PYQ Alert
TNPSC frequently tests little vs a little in Fill in the Blanks and Error Spotting. The negative/positive distinction is a classic trap. This has appeared in Group 4 (2015, 2018) and Group 2 (2016) papers.
Section 05
Adjectives of Number
Core Rule
Adjectives of Number → Always with COUNTABLE Nouns
Adjectives of number specify how many of something or the position of something. They are always used with countable nouns.
Sub-TypeWhat It DoesExamplesNotes
Definite Numeral Adjective States exact count or position one apple, two books, first place, 25th person Cardinal: one, two, three (how many)
Ordinal: first, second, third (position)
Indefinite Numeral Adjective Approximate count — noun is countable but exact number unknown few dogs, many letters, several students, some boys Dogs & letters ARE countable, but we don't know exact number
Distributive Numeral Adjective Refers to each/every member individually Each student must take a bath. / Every Indian must perform their duty. When used as ADJECTIVE (before noun), subsequent pronoun is plural (their)
⚡ Critical Fact — Distributive: Adjective vs Pronoun Pronoun Choice
Distributive PRONOUN (each/every standing alone) → use singular pronoun later in sentence: "Each of the students must bring his/her book."
Distributive ADJECTIVE (each/every before a noun) → use plural pronoun later: "Every Indian must perform their duty."
This subtle difference is a HIGH-VALUE TNPSC trap.
🪤 TNPSC Trap
Quantity vs Number Confusion:
"I ate some sugars" ✗ — sugar is uncountable → "I ate some sugar" ✓ (Quantity)
"Some water are available" ✗ — water is uncountable → singular verb ✓
"Some boys are athletic" ✓ — boys are countable → Indefinite Numeral Adjective
🧠 Memory Trick
Can you COUNT it? → Number adjective. Can you only MEASURE it? → Quantity adjective.
COUNT = soldiers, dogs, letters, students → NUMBER
MEASURE = water, sugar, wheat, petrol, wealth → QUANTITY
Section 06
Demonstrative Adjectives
Golden Rule
If it stands alone = Pronoun. If it precedes a noun = Adjective.
"This is the table." → This stands alone = Demonstrative Pronoun
"This table is mine." → This qualifies the noun table = Demonstrative Adjective
DistanceSingularPluralExample (Adjective use)
NearthistheseThis phone is mine. / These tickets are cheap.
FarthatthoseThat kite is red. / Those blankets are warm.
🪤 TNPSC Trap
Sentences like "That took forever" or "These are confusing" → Demonstrative Pronoun (no noun follows).
Sentences like "That phone is expensive" or "These books are heavy" → Demonstrative Adjective (noun follows immediately).
The test: Does a noun come immediately after? Yes = Adjective. No = Pronoun.
🏆 PYQ Alert
Error Spotting questions regularly include sentences where this/that/these/those is used incorrectly — particularly number agreement errors like "These book" instead of "These books". Appeared in Group 4 (2019, 2022).
Section 07
Interrogative Adjectives
Definition
Modifies a noun AND asks a question simultaneously
Interrogative adjectives perform two functions: (1) they qualify/modify a noun, and (2) they ask a question.
Key words: what · which · whose · where · why · how
FormPronoun (stands alone)Adjective (before noun)
which"Which is your book?" (stands alone, no noun follows)"Which book is yours?" (modifies 'book')
what"What happened?" (pronoun)"What pen will they give us?" (adjective)
whose"Whose is this?" (pronoun)"Whose lunch box is this?" (adjective)
⚡ Critical Fact
Interrogative PRONOUN: Can stand alone. Followed by a VERB.
Interrogative ADJECTIVE: Cannot stand alone. Must be followed by a NOUN.
Rule: Interrogative Pronoun → Verb follows. Interrogative Adjective → Noun follows.
🪤 TNPSC Trap
"Whose pen will they give us?" → whose modifies pen → Interrogative Adjective (also possessive sense).
Students often mark whose as only a possessive pronoun. When it precedes a noun, it is an interrogative adjective with possessive meaning.

Words ending in -ly that are ADJECTIVES (not adverbs): friendly, lovely, lonely, lively, deadly, elderly, orderly, cowardly, motherly, fatherly, heavenly

→ thoroughly is an adverb. Here we need an adjective to modify study (a noun).
→ Correct: "despite his thorough study."

Section 09
Degrees of Comparison
DegreeUsageFormationSignal Words
PositiveSimple statement, no comparisonBase form of adjectiveas…as / so…as
ComparativeComparing TWO persons / thingsAdjective + -er OR more + adjectivethan
SuperlativeComparing THREE or moreThe + Adjective + -est OR the most + adjectivethe (article mandatory)
Formation Rules
How to Form Comparative and Superlative
Rule A — Regular (add -er / -est):
dark → darker → darkest · light → lighter → lightest · high → higher → highest · warm → warmer → warmest

Rule B — Ends in -e (add just -r / -st):
nice → nicer → nicest · close → closer → closest · large → larger → largest

Rule C — Short word ending in consonant (double consonant + er/est):
sad → sadder → saddest · big → bigger → biggest · hot → hotter → hottest · fat → fatter → fattest

Rule D — Ends in -y (change y → i + er/est):
easy → easier → easiest · heavy → heavier → heaviest · funny → funnier → funniest · lovely → lovelier → loveliest

Rule E — Long words / multi-syllable (use more / most):
famous → more famous → most famous · precious → more precious → most precious · handsome → more handsome → most handsome · beautiful → more beautiful → most beautiful · exciting → more exciting → most exciting
⚡ Critical Fact — Irregular Comparisons (Must Memorise)
PositiveComparativeSuperlative
goodbetterbest
bad / illworseworst
littlelessleast
many / muchmoremost
farfarther / furtherfarthest / furthest
latelater / latterlatest / last
🪤 TNPSC Trap — "More" Redundancy
"I was more lonelier than I was in Canada." ✗
lonelier is already comparative. Adding more is REDUNDANT (double comparative).
Correct: "I was lonelier than I was in Canada." ✓

Similarly: "more bigger" ✗ · "more taller" ✗ · "most highest" ✗ (double superlative)
🪤 TNPSC Trap — Superlative vs Comparative (Two vs Three+)
"Of the two cities, the former is the biggest." ✗
Only TWO cities are being compared → must use comparative, not superlative.
Correct: "Of the two cities, the former is the bigger."

Rule: TWO items → -er (comparative) · THREE or more items → -est with "the" (superlative)
🪤 TNPSC Trap — Article "the" with Superlative
"He is a most popular film producer." ✗
Superlative degree ALWAYS takes the (definite article), never 'a'.
Correct: "He is the most popular film producer."
🪤 TNPSC Trap — Holier/Holiest with Ganges
"Everyone agrees that the Ganga is holier of all rivers of India." ✗
When comparing to ALL others, use: holier than any other river in India (comparative) OR the holiest of all rivers (superlative). Mixing holier with of all is the error — of all signals superlative context.
Correct: "The Ganga is holier than any other river in India." OR "The Ganga is the holiest of all rivers in India."
🧠 Memory Trick
THAN = 2 things (comparative). THE + -EST = 3+ things (superlative).
My painting is GOOD (positive) → Melanie's is BETTER (comparative, 2 compared) → Andrew's is THE BEST (superlative, 3 compared).
Count the items: 2 → THAN · 3+ → THE
🏆 PYQ Alert
Degree of comparison errors (particularly double comparative, wrong use of than/of, missing article "the" with superlative) are the MOST frequently tested adjective errors in TNPSC from 2012–2024. At least 1–2 questions per exam paper.
Section 10
All Adjective Rules — With Exam Examples
Rule 1
Both adjectives in a comparison must be in the same degree
Error: "He married a girl who was more tall and lovelier than he himself."
lovelier = comparative, but tall = positive → mismatch.
Correct: "…who was more taller — wait — who was taller and lovelier than he himself." (both comparative)
Rule 2
Comparative: TWO things. Superlative: THREE or more.
Error: "Of the two cities, the former is the biggest."
Correct: "Of the two cities, the former is the bigger."
Rule 3
No double comparative / double superlative
Error: "I was more lonelier than I was in Canada."
Correct: "I was lonelier than I was in Canada."

Error: "Her father told her it would be all the more better…"
Correct: Remove "all the more" → "…it would be better…"
Rule 4
Absolute adjectives cannot be compared
Some adjectives represent absolute qualities that cannot logically have degrees: square · round · perfect · eternal · universal · unique · infinite
Error: "This is a more perfect specimen." ✗
Correct: "This is the most perfect specimen I ever saw." (only superlative allowed, as exception for emphasis)
Rule 5
"The" + adjective = Noun (takes plural verb)
When the is placed before an adjective and no noun follows, the adjective functions as a noun and takes a plural verb.
"The rich feel the law can be turned and twisted." (rich = rich people)
"The blind / The poor / The injured" → all take plural verbs.

Error: "Honest are rewarded sooner or later." ✗
Correct: "The honest are rewarded sooner or later." ✓
Rule 6
Adjectives ending in -ior (superior, inferior, senior, junior) use "to" not "than"
Error: "Akshay is senior than Meenu." ✗
Correct: "Akshay is senior to Meenu." ✓

Words: superior, inferior, senior, junior, prior, anterior, posterior → always use to
Error from lecture: "Reka is junior than me." ✗ → "Reka is junior to me." ✓
Rule 7
Comparing qualities of the SAME person → do not use comparative degree
Error: "Rahul is luckier than hard working." ✗ (comparing Rahul's own luck to his own hard work)
Correct: "Rahul is more lucky than hard working." ✓
Rule: When two qualities of the SAME subject are compared, use more + positive adjective (never -er form).
Rule 8
As…as → Positive degree. Not so…as → Positive degree.
Between as…as and so…as, the adjective must be in the positive (base) form, never comparative.
"Hissar is as hot as Ganganagar in May." ✓ (NOT hotter)
"Bhopal is not so developed as Delhi." ✓ (NOT more developed)
Rule 9
later/latest vs latter/last
later = comparative of late (refers to TIME) → "He came later than I expected."
latest = superlative of late (refers to TIME — most recent) → "This is the latest news."
latter = the second of TWO things mentioned → "Of the two, I prefer the latter."
last = the final one of THREE or more → "The last player could not bat as he was injured."
🏆 PYQ Alert
Rule 6 (superior/inferior/senior/junior + "to") has appeared in TNPSC Group 1 Prelim (2015, 2018) and Group 4 (2017, 2020). Classic trap: students reflexively write "than" because comparative degree uses "than".

⚡ Critical Fact
"A little knowledge is a very dangerous thing." → a little = some (positive — there IS some knowledge, but it's dangerous)
"There is little hope of his recovery." → little = almost no hope (negative)
"There is a little hope of his recovery." → a little = some hope (positive)
The difference of one article (a) completely changes the meaning!
🪤 TNPSC Trap
"There are a few trains to cater to the ever-growing population of the city." ← This seems positive but the context is negative (insufficient trains). The correct word should be few (not enough trains, negative meaning).
Lesson: Read the context, not just the word. The surrounding sentence determines which form is correct.
🧠 Memory Trick — The "A" Rule
"A" = Alive (there is SOMETHING)
A little = alive (some exists) · A few = alive (some exist)
No "a" = Almost absent (nearly nothing)
Little = barely alive · Few = barely present
"The" = Total (ALL of the small amount)
The little = all of that tiny amount · The few = all of those few
Section 12
Order of Adjectives in a Sentence
⚡ Critical Fact — Fixed Order Must Be Followed
When multiple adjectives modify a noun, they must appear in a specific sequence. This is tested in Sentence Rearrangement and Error Spotting.
The ORDER: Determiner → Opinion → Size → Age → Shape → Colour → Origin → Material → Purpose → NOUN
DOSASCOMP + Noun (memory formula)
Determiner (a, the, some, my) → Opinion (cute, boring, lovely) → Size (little, huge, big) → Age (ancient, new, old) → Shape (round, square) → Colour (red, golden) → Origin (Portuguese, French) → Material (wooden, silk, plastic) → Purpose (wall, sleeping) → NOUN
SentenceAnalysisCorrect Order
"saw a cute little white cat"cute (opinion) + little (size) + white (colour) + cat (noun)✓ Already correct order
"ancient Portuguese synagogue"ancient (age) + Portuguese (origin) + synagogue (noun)✓ Already correct
"soft red silk sarees"soft (texture/opinion) + red (colour) + silk (material) + sarees (noun)✓ Already correct
"huge wooden wall cupboard"huge (size) + wooden (material) + wall (purpose/location) + cupboard (noun)✓ Correct
"silk pink dotted kurtha"silk (material) placed BEFORE colour and pattern"pink dotted silk kurtha" — colour before material
"tiny little golden red retriever puppy"tiny (size) + little (size) + golden red (colour) + retriever (origin/breed) + puppy (noun)✓ Correct
🪤 TNPSC Trap
"We saw him wearing a silk pink dotted kurtha." ✗
Colour (pink) must come before material (silk): "a pink dotted silk kurtha" ✓
Memory: Colour before Material always.
🧠 Memory Trick — DOSASCOMP
Determiner · Opinion · Size · Age · Shape · Colour · Origin · Material · Purpose → NOUN
Sentence to remember: "Do Old Stupid Ants Snore? Cats Often Munch Peas!"

Order of Adjectives appeared in TNPSC Group 1 Prelim (2016) and Group 2A (2018) as Sentence Rearrangement / Error Identification. Know DOSASCOMP cold.

🎯 Examiner's Trap MCQ — The Classic Confusion
Question: "Everyone agrees that the Ganga is _____ river in India."
A) holier than any B) the most holiest C) holiest of all the D) the holiest of all

Most students choose: C (holiest of all the) — seems superlative and grammatically complete.
Correct answer: D — "the holiest of all" → superlative requires the before it + of all (not "of all the") when referring to a class in general.
Why B is wrong: "the most holiest" = double superlative (most + -est).
Why A is wrong: "holier than any" is correct English but without "other" it implies the Ganga is compared to ITSELF.
Section 14
Quick MCQ Drill — 10 Questions
Q1. Identify the error: "He failed to find the solution despite his thoroughly study of the book."
A) failed to find
B) solution despite
C) thoroughly study
D) of the book
✅ Answer: C — thorough study
📌 Why: study is a noun here, so it needs an adjective (thorough), not an adverb (thoroughly).
🪤 Trap: Students see -ly and assume it's correct adverb usage, not noticing "study" is the noun being modified.
Q2. Choose the correct sentence:
A) Reka is junior than me.
B) Reka is junior to me.
C) Reka is more junior than me.
D) Reka is most junior of me.
✅ Answer: B — junior to me
📌 Why: Adjectives ending in -ior (junior, senior, superior, inferior) use "to", never "than".
🪤 Trap: Comparative degree → "than" — but -ior words are exceptions.
Q3. Which sentence uses degrees correctly?
A) Of the two sisters, Mary is the tallest.
B) Of the two sisters, Mary is the taller.
C) Of the two sisters, Mary is most tall.
D) Of the two sisters, Mary is more tallest.
✅ Answer: B — taller
📌 Why: TWO items being compared → comparative degree (-er). Superlative (-est) needs 3+ items.
🪤 Trap: "the" before the adjective makes students think it's superlative. But "the taller of the two" is correct comparative usage.
Q4. Identify the error in: "He offered me a steamed cup of soup."
A) He offered
B) me a
C) steamed cup
D) of soup
✅ Answer: C — should be "steaming cup"
📌 Why: The cup is actively giving off steam (ongoing action) → present participle steaming. Steamed = already cooked/steamed in the past.
🪤 Trap: Both seem fine, but context (the cup is hot and steaming as it's brought) demands -ing form.
Q5. Fill in the blank: "There is ___ hope of his recovery." (meaning: almost no hope)
A) a little
B) little
C) the little
D) some
✅ Answer: B — little
📌 Why: little (without "a") = negative, almost nothing. a little = positive, some exists.
🪤 Trap: Students choose "a little" thinking it sounds more natural and polite — completely reverses the meaning!
Q6. Identify error: "I was more lonelier than I was in Canada."
A) more lonelier
B) than I was
C) in Canada
D) No error
✅ Answer: A — should be just "lonelier"
📌 Why: lonelier is already comparative. Adding more creates a double comparative, which is wrong.
🪤 Trap: Students miss this because "more lonelier" sounds emphatic to non-native ears.
Q7. "Which book is yours?" — The word 'which' here is:
A) Interrogative Pronoun
B) Interrogative Adjective
C) Demonstrative Adjective
D) Possessive Pronoun
✅ Answer: B — Interrogative Adjective
📌 Why: which directly precedes and modifies the noun book → Interrogative Adjective.
🪤 Trap: "Which is your book?" → Pronoun (no noun follows). "Which book is yours?" → Adjective (noun 'book' follows).
Q8. Choose the correct order: ___ kurtha for the function.
A) silk pink dotted
B) dotted silk pink
C) pink dotted silk
D) pink silk dotted
✅ Answer: C — pink dotted silk
📌 Why: Order = Colour (pink) → Pattern/Opinion (dotted) → Material (silk) → Noun. Colour before material.
🪤 Trap: "silk pink" sounds natural in Indian English but violates the standard adjective order rule.
Q9. "I bought the revise edition of the book." The error is:
A) I bought
B) the
C) revise edition
D) of the book
✅ Answer: C — should be "revised edition"
📌 Why: The edition has already been revised (past participle as adjective) → revised, not revise.
🪤 Trap: Students confuse verb "to revise" (bare form) with the adjectival past participle "revised".
Q10. "He is comparatively weaker in Science." The error is:
A) comparatively
B) weaker in
C) Science
D) No error
✅ Answer: A — remove "comparatively"
📌 Why: weaker is already in the comparative degree. Adding comparatively is redundant. Correct: "He is weaker in Science."
🪤 Trap: comparatively sounds formal and correct, so students skip it. The adjective weaker already carries the comparative meaning.
Section 15
Master Cheat Card — 60-Second Revision
Adj of Quality
Describes nature/characteristics. All noun types. Watch: -ly ≠ always adverb (lovely, friendly = adjectives)
Adj of Quantity
UNCOUNTABLE nouns only. Keywords: much, little, a little, enough, sufficient, some, all, no
Adj of Number
COUNTABLE nouns. Definite (one/first), Indefinite (few/many), Distributive (each/every)
Demonstrative
This/That/These/Those + NOUN = Adjective. Alone = Pronoun. Near singular=this, far singular=that
Interrogative
What/Which/Whose + NOUN = Adjective. Alone + Verb = Pronoun.
-ing vs -ed Adj
-ing = causing feeling (exciting news). -ed = feeling the effect (excited student). Steaming cup ✓ NOT steamed cup
Degrees Rule
2 items = comparative (-er / more). 3+ items = superlative (the -est / the most). Never double: more lonelier ✗
Irregular Forms
good→better→best. bad→worse→worst. little→less→least. many/much→more→most. far→farther→farthest
-ior Rule
Senior/Junior/Superior/Inferior + TO (never than). "She is senior TO me" ✓
little vs a little
little = negative (almost none). a little = positive (some). the little = all of the small amount.
few vs a few
few = negative (almost none). a few = positive (some). the few = all of those few.
Adj Order (DOSASCOMP)
Determiner → Opinion → Size → Age → Shape → Colour → Origin → Material → Purpose → NOUN. Colour before Material always.
As…as rule
Between as…as and so…as: always POSITIVE degree. "as hot as" not "as hotter as".
The + Adj = Noun
The rich / The blind / The honest = plural noun → takes plural verb. "The honest are rewarded" ✓
Superlative + THE
Superlative ALWAYS needs "the": "He is THE most popular" ✓. "He is A most popular" ✗.
later/latter/latest/last
later=time (comparative). latest=most recent (superlative). latter=2nd of two. last=final of 3+.
much vs many
much = uncountable (how much water). many = countable (how many people).
Absolute Adjectives
perfect, unique, round, square, infinite, eternal = cannot be compared normally. Avoid "more perfect" ✗.
