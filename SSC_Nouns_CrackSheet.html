Definition & Overview — What Is a Noun?

A noun is one of the two primary building blocks of English (the other being verbs). It is the name given to a person, place, thing, animal, idea, feeling, or concept. Every sentence requires at least one noun to convey meaning.

✅ TIP — MEMORY HOOK Everything around you right now is a noun: pen, board, chair, table. Even abstract ideas like fear, democracy, luck are nouns. If you can name it, it's a noun.

Six Types of Nouns — Complete Reference

#TypeDefinitionExamplesSSC Relevance
1 Common Noun General name for any person, place, or thing in a class city, chair, dog, teacher, pen, student Basis for all plural rules; error spotting
2 Proper Noun Specific name of a particular person, place, or thing. Always capitalised. Chennai, Ravi, Diwali, Tajmahal, India, Monday, January Capitalisation errors; article use before proper nouns
3 Collective Noun Name for a group of persons, animals, or things treated as one unit family, class, committee, flock, herd, choir, gang, jury S-V agreement; collective noun takes singular verb (usually)
4 Concrete Noun Something perceivable by the five senses (see, hear, smell, taste, touch) pen, apple, music (heard), perfume (smelled), stone (touched) Most common nouns in sentences are concrete
5 Abstract Noun Has no physical form; exists only as a concept, idea, feeling, quality, state, or event fear, honesty, democracy, luxury, freedom, childhood, opportunity, death, marriage Uncountable rules; cannot be pluralised or preceded by a/an
6 Compound Noun Two or more words combined to form one new noun. Written as one word, hyphenated, or two words. toothpaste, fruit juice, ice cream, crossroad, stepmother, mother-in-law Pluralisation rule — only the HEAD noun is pluralised
⚡ SSC TRAP — COMMON vs PROPER city = Common Noun (lowercase) | Chennai = Proper Noun (uppercase)
library = Common | Anna Centenary Library = Proper
park = Common | Lal Bagh Botanical Garden = Proper
Rule: Once you give a specific name to something, it becomes Proper.

Singular → Plural: All 13 Rules

RULE 01

General Rule — Add -s

For most nouns, simply add -s to form the plural.

pen → pens | mountain → mountains | river → rivers | computer → computers | player → players

RULE 02

Nouns ending in -s / -ss / -sh / -ch / -x / -z → Add -es

SingularPluralSingularPlural
busbusesclassclasses
brushbrushesbranchbranches
churchchurchesbeachbeaches
boxboxessandwichsandwiches
witchwitchesflashflashes

RULE 03

Nouns ending in consonant + y → Change y to i, add -es

baby→babies | family→families | story→stories | fairy→fairies | puppy→puppies | city→cities
⚡ SSC TRAP — vowel + y exception If the letter before y is a vowel, just add -s (do NOT change y to i):
runway→runways | chimney→chimneys | trolley→trolleys | valley→valleys | monkey→monkeys

RULE 04

Nouns ending in -f / -fe → Change to -ves

leaf→leaves | shelf→shelves | wolf→wolves | wife→wives | knife→knives | life→lives | half→halves
⚡ SSC TRAP — -f exceptions (just add -s) roof→roofs | chief→chiefs | cliff→cliffs | belief→beliefs | grief→griefs
Also irregular: handkerchief→handkerchiefs/handkerchieves (both accepted)

RULE 05

Nouns ending in consonant + o → Add -es

hero→heroes | mango→mangoes | potato→potatoes | mosquito→mosquitoes | cargo→cargoes | echo→echoes
⚡ SSC TRAP — vowel + o, or short foreign words, just add -s radio→radios | studio→studios | piano→pianos | zoo→zoos | bamboo→bamboos | dynamo→dynamos

RULE 06

A / An rule before Singular Countable Nouns

Use a before consonant sounds. Use an before vowel sounds (not letters — sounds).

WordFirst SoundArticleExample
artistvowel /æ/anan artist
insectvowel /ɪ/anan insect
umbrellavowel /ʌ/anan umbrella
hourvowel /aʊ/ (h is silent)anan hour
honourvowel /ɒ/ (h is silent)anan honour
heirvowel /eə/ (h is silent)anan heir
uniformconsonant /juː/ (y-sound)aa uniform
unitconsonant /juː/ (y-sound)aa unit
universityconsonant /juː/ (y-sound)aa university
🔴 CRITICAL RULE The article is determined by the SOUND, not the letter. hour starts with 'h' (consonant letter) but is pronounced /aʊər/ → use an hour. uniform starts with 'u' (vowel letter) but is pronounced /juː/ → use a uniform.

RULE 08

Same Form — Singular = Plural (Zero Plurals)

These nouns do NOT change form in the plural.

NounSingular UsePlural UseException Note
sheepone sheepmany sheepAlways sheep
deera deerseveral deerAlways deer
fisha fishmany fishfishes = different species
aircraftan aircrafttwo aircraftAlways aircraft
salmona salmon100 salmonAlways salmon
seriesa seriestwo seriesAlways series
speciesa speciesmany speciesAlways species
🔴 CRITICAL RULE — fish vs. fishes fish = plural of ONE species (I bought 10 fish). fishes = multiple different species (The ocean has many fishes). This distinction is a favourite SSC trap.

RULE 09

Always Plural (Pluralia Tantum) — Paired / Symmetric Objects

These nouns exist only in the plural. They refer to objects with two matching parts or are always used in pairs.

GroupNounsCorrect Usage
Clothing (paired legs)trousers, jeans, shorts, tights, pyjamas, pantsa pair of trousers
Eyewearglasses, spectacles, goggles, binocularsa pair of glasses
Tools (two handles)scissors, pliers, tongs, clippersa pair of scissors
Footwear (pair)shoes, sandals, boots, slippers, socksa pair of shoes
Cookingchopsticksa pair of chopsticks
⚡ SSC TRAP You cannot say "a scissor" or "a trouser". Always use a pair of scissors / trousers. These take a plural verb: "My trousers are torn."

RULE 11

Nouns ending in -ful → Add -s after -ful

cupsful ❌→cupfuls ✓
spoonful→spoonfuls | handful→handfuls | glassful→glassfuls | bucketful→bucketfuls
🔴 CRITICAL RULE The -s goes AFTER the whole word: cupfuls, never cupsful. The word is a single unit; the -ful suffix makes it a new noun.

RULE 12

Hyphenated Nouns with Numbers → Always use Singular Head

When a noun phrase with a number is used as a modifier (adjective) before another noun, keep the first noun singular.

Wrong ❌Correct ✓Rule

a 20-men committeea 20-man committeeUsed as adjective → singular
a 10-pounds notea 10-pound noteUsed as adjective → singular
a 5-years-old childa 5-year-old childUsed as adjective → singular
a 2-miles walka 2-mile walkUsed as adjective → singular

RULE 13

Plural-looking Nouns that are Singular — Take Singular Verb

These nouns end in -s or look plural but are grammatically SINGULAR. Always use a singular verb with them.

CategoryNounsCorrect Verb Form
Academic Subjectsmathematics, physics, economics, civics, statistics, linguisticsMathematics is difficult.

Games / Sportsbilliards, athletics, darts, gymnasticsBilliards is played all over.
NewsnewsThe news was good.
Othermeans, series, innings, politics, ethicsThe series is interesting.
🔴 CRITICAL RULE — The #1 SSC Error Spotting Target
Mathematics are my favourite subject. ❌
Mathematics is my favourite subject. ✓
The news were very depressing. ❌
The news was very depressing. ✓
Linguistics are the study of language. ❌
Linguistics is the study of language. ✓

// SECTION 05

Possessive Forms of Nouns

The possessive form shows ownership or association. It is formed using an apostrophe + s ('s).

CaseRuleExampleMeaning
Singular nounAdd 'sRavi's pen | Peter's bed | Dad's cookingBelongs to one person
Plural noun ending in -sAdd ' only (after s)rats' tails | horses' hooves | teachers' booksBelongs to many
Plural noun NOT ending in -sAdd 'schildren's room | men's voices | women's rightsIrregular plural possessive
Two owners, shared itemAdd 's to the LAST name onlyKatie and Mike's house (one shared house)Joint ownership
Two owners, separate itemsAdd 's to BOTH namesJoe's and Sara's bags (each has their own)Separate ownership
Name ending in -sAdd 's OR just 'James's bike = James' bikeBoth acceptable
🔴 CRITICAL RULE — Avoid stacked possessives Instead of Tom's father's hat ❌ (clunky), prefer: the hat of Tom's father ✓
For complex possessives, restructure: Paul's teacher's house → the house of Paul's teacher
⚡ SSC TRAP — CHAINED POSSESSIVES SSC may give: "He told me that it was his friend's Ravi's house." This is WRONG. Correct: "He told me that it was his friend Ravi's house."

Possessive Practice Sentences from Lecture

This is Susan and Jeni room. ❌→This is Susan and Jeni's room. ✓ (joint)
He gave informations. ❌→He gave some information. ✓ (uncountable)
The horse's tail is surrounded by every insects. ❌→The horse's tail is surrounded by every insect. ✓

Countable & Uncountable Nouns

FeatureCountableUncountable
Can be counted?Yes (1, 2, 3…)No
Has plural form?Yes: pen → pensNo: rice → rice (NOT rices)
Use a/an?Yes: a pen, an appleNo: ~~a rice~~ | a bowl of rice ✓
Use many/few?Yes: many books, few pensNo: ~~many water~~ | much/little water ✓
Use much/little?No (use many/few)Yes: much rain, little sugar
Use some/any?Yes (plural): some booksYes: some water, any milk
Quantifier needed?Not for plural: I like bananasYes: a cup of coffee / a bag of sugar

⚡ SSC TRAP — little vs. a little

I have a little water. ✓ (= some water — positive meaning)
I have little work to do. ❌ (= virtually no work)
I have a little work to do. ✓ (= some work)
Same pattern: few (negative — nearly none) vs. a few (positive — some).

// SECTION 08

2026 Prediction Vault

HIGH PROBABILITY
Uncountable noun used in plural form
furnitures / informations / advices / luggages / jewelleries — These appear in nearly every SSC paper cycle. Expect at least one uncountable error in Error Spotting.
All the furnitures in his room are expensive. ❌→All the furniture in his room is expensive. ✓
HIGH PROBABILITY
Compound noun — wrong pluralisation (in-law series)
SSC CGL 2024/25 trend: brothers-in-law / mothers-in-law / commanders-in-chief / members of parliament. The error is always adding -s to the preposition or the last word.
He invited all his brother-in-laws. ❌→He invited all his brothers-in-law. ✓
HIGH PROBABILITY
Plural-form singular subject (mathematics/news) + wrong verb
Classic SVA error. Mathematics are / Physics have / News were — all wrong. These are expected in Sentence Improvement sections.
The news were very disturbing. ❌→The news was very disturbing. ✓
RISING TREND
little vs. a little — Cloze Test
In fill-in-the-blank, choose between little (almost nothing) and a little (some). Rising frequency in recent CHSL papers.
She has a little money left. ✓ (= some money)
She has little hope. ✓ (= almost no hope)
RISING TREND
Number + noun modifier — singular form required
"a 3-days workshop" type errors increasing in Tier II. The number-noun combo always takes singular form when used as an adjective.
MEDIUM
Collective Noun — animal group vocabulary
Fill-in questions: "A ___ of lions / a ___ of ships / a ___ of fish." Medium frequency but highly scoreable if prepared.
🔴 EXAMINER'S TRAP #1 — The Single Most Likely Mistake Using a/an based on the LETTER (not sound): writing "a hour" instead of "an hour", or "an university" instead of "a university". The sound rule for articles catches most students. Similarly, a/an + uncountable noun (e.g., "an information") is instantly wrong.
