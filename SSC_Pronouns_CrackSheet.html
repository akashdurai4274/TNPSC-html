Foundation · Definition
What is a Pronoun?
CORE DEFINITION
PRO + NOUN = "Instead of a Noun"

A pronoun is a word used in place of a noun to avoid repetitive use of the same noun. It is introduced after the noun (antecedent) has been established at least once.

⚠️ Critical Rule — Antecedent Agreement

A pronoun must agree with its antecedent (the noun it replaces) in person, number, and gender. This is the #1 source of errors in SSC Error Spotting.

Miss Milling stood at Miss Milling's classroom door and greeted Miss Milling's third-period students as Miss Milling's students walked into the classroom.
→ All repetitions replaced with pronouns:
Miss Milling stood at her classroom door and greeted her third-period students as they walked into the classroom.

Type 1 · Most Tested
Personal Pronouns

Personal pronouns refer to specific persons or things. They have two positional forms: Subjective (Nominative Case) — used as the subject of a verb — and Objective (Accusative Case) — used as the object of a verb or preposition.

PersonNumberSubjectObjectPossessive Adj.Possessive Pro.Reflexive
1stSingularImemyminemyself
1stPluralweusouroursourselves
2ndSing./Pl.youyouyouryoursyourself / yourselves
3rdSing. (M)hehimhishishimself
3rdSing. (F)sheherherhersherself
3rdSing. (N)ititits—itself
3rdPluraltheythemtheirtheirsthemselves
⚠️ Critical Rule — Subject vs. Object Position
  • Use Subject pronouns (I, he, she, we, they) as the doer of the action.
  • Use Object pronouns (me, him, her, us, them) after verbs and prepositions.
  • In comparisons using than/as, always use the subject pronoun. "She is far better than he" (not him) — because "he [is]" is the implied complete clause.

Practice Sentences from the Lecture

Lisa likes cats. She likes to stroke them.
"She" = subject; "them" = object (replaces "cats")
My name is Michael. I am 14. My father works hard. He is a doctor. My sister is older than me. She is 12.
Was it I who had to face the music?
After linking verbs (is/was), use Subject pronoun, not object.
As a student of literature, she is far better than he.
"him" is WRONG here — comparison clause requires subject pronoun.
🪤 SSC Trap — "It is I" vs. "It is me"

Formally/grammatically correct: "It is I" (subject pronoun after linking verb). SSC tests this. Students instinctively write "It is me" (colloquially acceptable, but grammatically debated). In SSC Error Spotting, treat "It is me" as an error and replace with "It is I."

Type 2 · Critical Distinction
Possessive Pronouns vs. Possessive Adjectives
⚠️ Critical Rule — The Most Tested Distinction

This distinction appears almost every year in SSC exams.

  • Possessive Adjective (my, your, his, her, its, our, their) — always accompanies a noun. It modifies the noun. "my book"
  • Possessive Pronoun (mine, yours, his, hers, ours, theirs) — stands alone. It replaces the noun entirely. "This book is mine."
  • NO apostrophe is ever used with possessive pronouns. your's / their's = always WRONG.
Possessive AdjectiveUsagePossessive PronounUsage
mymy book (+ noun)mineThis book is mine.
youryour penyoursWhere is yours?
hishis carhisThe car is his.
herher cathersIs this cat hers?
itsits tail—(no standalone form)
ourour houseoursThe house is ours.
theirtheir lunchtheirsWe had lunch but they had theirs.

Practice Sentences from the Lecture

You said it's your problem, but I said it's mine.
"mine" = possessive pronoun replacing "my problem"
Your problems are the same as mine. → Corrected: Yours are the same problems as mine.
Tara has lost her cat. Is this cat hers?
I see our car, but where is yours?
🪤 SSC Trap — Letter Closing "Yours faithfully"

Your's faithfully = WRONG (apostrophe with possessive pronoun is never allowed)
Yours faithfully = CORRECT. This is a classic SSC question. "Yours" here is a possessive pronoun, not a contraction. Never use an apostrophe with mine, yours, his, hers, ours, theirs, its.

🏆 PYQ Alert — Appeared in SSC CGL 2019, 2022

Sentence: "The hotels of Ravi are more luxurious than Rohan." → Error: comparing hotels with a person.
Correction: "…than those of Rohan." — Use demonstrative pronoun "those of" to compare equivalent nouns (hotels with hotels).

Type 3 · Frequently Misused
Reflexive & Intensive Pronouns
REFLEXIVE
Action reflects back on subject

The subject and object are the same entity. The reflexive pronoun is essential — removing it changes or destroys the meaning.

She hurt herself.
Remove "herself" → sentence loses meaning
INTENSIVE (EMPHATIC)
Used for emphasis only

Same -self/-selves form, but used purely for emphasis. Removing it does NOT change the core meaning.

Hana herself made the dinner reservation.
Remove "herself" → meaning remains clear
⚠️ Critical Rule — "Myself" cannot replace "I" or "me"
  • You and myself will enjoy the party. → WRONG (no antecedent "I" earlier in sentence)
  • You and I will enjoy the party. → CORRECT
  • Reflexive pronouns cannot be used as plain subject or object pronouns. They require the base pronoun to appear earlier.

Verbs that REQUIRE Reflexive Pronouns (SSC Hot List)

Verbs that DO NOT use Reflexive Pronouns (Exception List)

🪤 SSC Trap — Avoid Reflexive with Routine Actions

For routine bodily actions, reflexive pronouns are not used in standard English.

He washed himself in cold water. → Standard: He washed in cold water.
He always shaved himself before going out. → He always shaved before going out.
Michael dressed himself for the party. → Michael dressed for the party.

Exception: "He is old enough to wash herself" — here the reflexive IS needed because it emphasizes the capability (emphatic use).

🏆 PYQ Alert

Sentence: "He bathed himself in the river." — The error is himself. Bathing is a routine action; reflexive pronoun is redundant. Remove it. This pattern appeared in SSC CHSL 2021.

Type 4
Reciprocal Pronouns
CORE RULE

Reciprocate = to return an action. Reciprocal pronouns express a mutual/shared action or relationship.

  • Each other → used when exactly 2 people/things are involved
  • One another → used when more than 2 people/things are involved
SituationUseExample
2 peopleeach otherJohn and Kate love each other.
3+ peopleone anotherThey recited poetry to one another.
2 peopleone anotherThe two sisters shouted at one another in public. ✗
The two sisters shouted at one another in public. → Correct: shouted at each other.
🪤 SSC Trap — Count the People

SSC will give you a sentence with "each other" or "one another" and change the number of participants. Always count the people/things before choosing. Two sisters = 2 people = "each other." A class of students = 3+ = "one another."

Type 5 · Most Complex
Relative Pronouns

A relative pronoun connects (relates) a clause or phrase to a noun or pronoun. It introduces an adjective clause that gives more information about the antecedent.

PronounRefers ToFunctionExample
whoPersons (subject)Subject of the relative clauseThe musician who wrote the song is French.
whomPersons (object)Object of the relative clauseI know the boy whom she met.
whosePersons / thingsPossessive relativeThe boy whose phone rang stood up.
whichAnimals / ThingsSubject or Object; also in comparisonsThe car which she bought is red.
thatPersons, animals, thingsUniversal; used in restrictive clauses12th Sept is the date that I was born.
⚠️ Critical Rule — WHO vs. WHOM
  • WHO = Subject pronoun (He/She equivalent). Ask: "Who did it?" → answer is the subject.
  • WHOM = Object pronoun (Him/Her equivalent). Ask: "Whom did I meet?" → answer is the object.
  • Test: Replace with he/him. If "he" fits → use WHO. If "him" fits → use WHOM.
The person who I met yesterday was my classmate.
Test: "I met him" → him fits → use WHOM
Correct: The person whom I met yesterday was my classmate.

WHICH vs. THAT — Key Distinction

ContextUse
Restrictive clause (essential info, no commas)that
Non-restrictive clause (extra info, with commas)which
Comparison between two people/animalswhich
Refers to a person (subject position)who / that
When antecedent is both person + thingthat
The chef did not know who of the servants broke the expensive dinner set.
Two servants → comparison → use WHICH
Correct: …which of the servants…
He was on the phone for hours together, who really irritated his parents.
"Who" refers to a thing (the action of being on the phone), not a person → use WHICH
Correct: …which really irritated his parents.
🏆 PYQ Alert — SSC CGL 2018, 2020, 2023

"whose" for possessive relative clauses is frequently tested. Pattern: "The boy whose back is red has to stand up." Students confuse this with "who's" (= who is). "Who's" ≠ "whose." SSC tests this apostrophe distinction.

DistanceSingularPlural

⚠️ Critical Rule — Demonstrative Pronoun vs. Demonstrative Adjective
  • Demonstrative Pronoun: Stands alone, replaces a noun. "This is a table."
  • Demonstrative Adjective: Modifies a noun. "This table is here."
  • Same words (this/that/these/those) — function determines the category.
🏆 PYQ Alert — "Those of" for Comparison

"The hotels of Ravi are more luxurious than Rohan." → Error: comparing hotels with a person.
Correction: "…than those of Rohan." — "those of" = the hotels of. This appeared in SSC CGL 2022 Tier-II.

Rule: When comparing possessions/attributes, use that of (singular) or those of (plural) to avoid faulty comparison.

Practice Sentences

This is my desk. That is my friend's house. [Demonstrative Pronouns]
These are my pets. Those are horses.
This table is here. [Demonstrative Adjective — "this" modifies "table"]
Type 7 · Verb Agreement Trap
Distributive Pronouns

Distributive pronouns refer to members of a group individually, not collectively. Although addressing a group, each member is considered separately and singularly.

PronounMeaningNumber of PeopleVerb
eachevery one individually2 or moreSingular
eitherone of the twoexactly 2Singular
neithernot one of the twoexactly 2Singular
⚠️ Critical Rule — Always Singular Verb
  • Each, either, neither → always take a singular verb
  • Each of the players is playing well. (NOT are)
  • Neither of the brothers has given the correct answer. (NOT have)
  • For 3 or more people, use none / no one / any (not either/neither)
🪤 SSC Trap — Either/Neither for TWO only

"None of the two boys came." → WRONG. "None" is for 3+.
"Neither of the two boys came." → CORRECT. For exactly 2.

Practice Sentences

Each of my friends has made a phone call to me today.
Each of the players is playing well.
Either Adam or Eve is guilty.
Neither of the brothers has given a correct answer.
Neither suggestion will be accepted.
Neither is suitable for the post.
Type 8
Indefinite Pronouns

Indefinite pronouns do not refer to a specific person, place, or thing. They begin with words like any-, every-, some-, no-.

CategoryPronounsVerb Agreement
Always Singularno one, nobody, nothing, anyone, anybody, anything, everyone, everybody, everything, someone, somebody, something, each, either, neither, oneSingular verb
Always Pluralseveral, few, both, manyPlural verb
Singular OR Pluralsome, any, more, most, all, noneDepends on context
⚠️ Critical Rule — "one" Pronoun Consistency

When you start a sentence with "one", you must maintain "one" throughout. Never switch to he/his/him midway.

One should keep one's promise. (NOT his promise)
One should do one's duty. (NOT their duty)
⚠️ Critical Rule — "All is well" vs. "All are here"
  • All is well → "all" refers to a concept/state → singular verb
  • They were all elated → "all" refers to a group of people → plural verb
  • Context determines singular/plural for: some, any, more, most, all, none

Pronoun-Verb Agreement with -body / -one / -thing

Everybody who was working in the company gave his or her contribution.
NOT "their" — "everybody" = singular
Everyone loves this song. [singular]
Nobody can do it. [singular verb]
None of the scholars of this class has passed.
Order of Pronouns · Most SSC-Tested Rule
The "231" and "123" Order Rules
⚠️ Critical Rule — Order When Something Negative / Bad / Blameworthy

When confessing a mistake, wrongdoing, or negative situation, put yourself first (responsibility):

1st Person → 2nd Person → 3rd Person
"I, you, and he are responsible."

I, you and he have committed this mistake.
⚠️ Critical Rule — Order When Something Positive / Praise / Award

When talking about something favourable, complimentary, or praiseworthy — put the other person first (politeness/courtesy):

2nd Person → 3rd Person → 1st Person
"You, he, and I won the award."

You, he and I have completed our studies.
You, Raj and I have completed our studies. (NOT Raj, I and you)
🪤 SSC Trap — The Arrangement Question

Previous Year Question pattern: "He, I and you shall arrange everything." — Find the error and rearrange.
Since it is a positive/neutral statement — order should be: "You, he and I shall arrange everything."
The error is at part A (He, I and you → wrong order). This appeared in multiple SSC papers.

Summary — The 231 / 123 Rule

SituationOrderMnemonic
Positive / Praise / Good news2 → 3 → 1"Young Tigers I-dentify" (You-They-I)
Negative / Mistake / Blame1 → 2 → 3"I went You went They went" (I confess first)
Advanced Rules · Tier II Focused
Either…Or / Neither…Nor — Agreement Rules
RULE A — Singular + Singular
Both nouns singular → Singular verb + Singular pronoun
Either Raj or Mohit will invest his money.
Neither Raj nor Sam could pass the test.
RULE B — Singular + Plural
Mixed → Verb agrees with the NEARER noun; Plural pronoun

Singular noun comes FIRST, plural noun comes SECOND. The verb/pronoun matches the closer (second) noun.

Either the leader or the associates were associated with the responsibility of citing the authority — their authorised message.
RULE C — 3+ Singular Nouns with Each/Every
Each/Every + Noun + and + Noun → Singular verb + Singular pronoun
Every learner and every teacher does his or her duty.
🪤 SSC Trap — "Neither of the two" vs "None of them"
  • "Neither" = only for exactly 2
  • "None" = for 3 or more
  • "None of the two friends has got the money back" → WRONG → "Neither of the two friends has got the money back"
  • "Neither of the three boys came" → WRONG → "None of the three boys came"
